# Claude Review — Mechanical Ethics Chapter 2 v0.1

Decision: Chapter 2 argues and can move forward, but needs cheap copyedit and book-level carry-forward warnings. Chapter 3 must stop winning easy.

---

Read it. It cleared the bar I set — this argues, it doesn't just orient. Attacking what still fails:

1. **Does it argue? Yes — but it wins too easily.** It has a real thesis ("uncertainty doesn't get you off the hook; it changes what the hook is"), a worked case, both failure modes named, a conclusion. That's a genuine argument chapter. The problem: **it never stages a real opponent.** The claim is framed so reasonably that almost no one resists it — nobody actually believes uncertainty *removes* responsibility. You've won an argument no one was having. The genuinely contestable territory — *where* the line is, what to do when "keep correction alive" and "protect against the worst harm" point in opposite directions — you gesture at ("both errors are real") and then resolve too smoothly. A sharp reader feels the opponent was made of straw.

2. **Central claim — clear, very. Defensible, yes, but safe.** Crisp and well-unpacked. The defensibility gap isn't the headline (it's nearly consensual); it's the *specific* content you load on: you assert "keep correction alive" is **the** first duty without arguing why it beats rivals (precautionary principle, minimise expected harm, defer to the most vulnerable). "First duty" claims a ranking you don't earn.

3. **Teacher/child case — works as a vehicle, but it's your riskiest choice and you lean on it.** Structurally it makes the two-error dilemma vivid, better than welfare/rent for this chapter. But: (a) suspected child abuse is the most emotionally loaded example available, and a sceptical reader registers that as the argument leaning on horror rather than reasoning; (b) it's a domain where your measured counsel ("don't make yourself the hero, keep it open, don't turn suspicion into certainty") is *genuinely contested* — safeguarding practice often says the opposite: report the suspicion, let trained people assess, don't sit on it. So you picked a case where your framework's advice is arguably *wrong*, and the chapter doesn't engage that. (c) Register fatigue: foreword, Ch1, Ch2 all ask the reader to feel for a vulnerable victim. The book has one emotional key. Either engage the safeguarding objection head-on, or pick a case where your counsel is *hard* rather than obviously wise — and vary the register.

4. **"Keep correction alive" — good content, overclaimed framing.** The unpacking is strong and concrete ("don't close more future than you have to… don't make the person carry the cost of your not-knowing"). But it's a *meta-duty* (preserve correctability), not an action-guide — in the teacher case it doesn't tell her whether to call social services. Calling it "the first duty" implies it resolves the live decision; it doesn't. Name it as a *constraint on how you act*, not a substitute for the judgement of what to do.

5. **Voice — holds, but the AI cadence is creeping back.** It's mostly you. But the synonym-piles and gong-lines you killed in Ch1 v0.2 have returned: "ill, frightened, tired, bullied, embarrassed, or simply having a bad week"; "overreact, underreact, rationalise, panic, freeze"; "Institutions do. Doctors do. Parents do. Teachers do. Engineers do. Governments do." Several per chapter, plus the one-line gongs ("That is the point." "Both errors are real." "Attention is not accusation."). You fixed this once; it regressed. Re-apply the same discipline.

6. **Human/AI partial-sight bridge — works, no flattening.** "Not the same kind of limitation, but they rhyme enough to matter" is exactly the right hedge. And the AI-confidence point — "AI can make partial sight look complete… a number is not certainty… measurement steals the uncertainty from the room," the 73% example — is the chapter's strongest *new* contribution. Keep all of it.

7. **What stops a reader:** the child case (emotional leverage / safeguarding bristle); the returning AI cadence (re-triggers "did a machine write this," fatal for *this* book); and the sharp reader noticing the contestable claim isn't really contested.
