# Chapter 2 — Partial Sight

Here is the claim I want to make in this chapter:

Uncertainty does not get you off the hook.

It changes what the hook is.

A lot of harm hides inside the sentence “we didn’t know.” Sometimes that sentence is true. Sometimes nobody did know enough. Sometimes the facts were incomplete, the signal was weak, and the person making the decision had to move anyway.

I am not interested in pretending otherwise.

People do have to act without the full picture. So do institutions. So will AIs, if they are allowed to act in the world.

The problem is not that we act under uncertainty.

The problem is what we allow uncertainty to excuse.

Take a difficult case.

A teacher notices that a child has changed. Not dramatically. Nothing that proves anything by itself. The child is quieter. Work is missing. They flinch once when an adult moves too quickly. They say they are fine.

The teacher does not know.

They do not know whether something is happening at home. They do not know whether the child is ill, frightened, or simply having a bad week. They do not know whether asking too directly will help or make things worse.

So what should they do?

This is where the case is deliberately uncomfortable. In real safeguarding, the answer is often not “sit with the uncertainty.” It may be: report the concern, follow the process, let trained people assess it. That objection is serious. A child should not have to wait while an adult performs philosophical caution.

So Mechanical Ethics cannot mean “do nothing until you are sure.”

But it also cannot mean “turn suspicion into fact because the possible harm is frightening.”

Both failures are real. One abandons the child to whatever may already be happening. The other can turn a weak signal into an accusation that damages people before anyone has properly looked.

That is the hard part.

Uncertainty does not give us a clean answer. It changes what responsible action has to preserve while the answer is still unclear.

In this case, that might mean the teacher does not become the investigator. They do not decide guilt. They do not dramatise themselves into the hero of the story. They record carefully, speak to the right safeguarding lead, pass on the concern through the proper route, and keep the child’s live safety in view.

That is not passivity.

It is action under partial sight.

The point is not that “keep correction alive” tells you exactly what to do. It does not. It is not a substitute for judgement, law, training, or domain knowledge.

It is a constraint on how you act.

Whatever action you take, do not close more future than you have to. Do not pretend the signal is stronger than it is. Do not wait so long that the harm you feared becomes permanent. Do not make the person inside the uncertainty carry the whole cost of your not knowing.

This is what I mean by partial sight.

You do not get the whole world. You get fragments. A change in behaviour. A missing piece of work. A sentence that does not sound right. None of that is enough to convict anyone. It may still be enough to require attention.

Attention is not accusation.

One of the easiest mistakes is to think there are only two modes: ignore it, or declare guilt. But most responsible action happens in the uncomfortable space between those two. You notice. You record carefully. You speak to someone with the right responsibility. You do not make the child carry the whole burden of proof. You do not turn uncertainty into certainty because certainty would make you feel less exposed.

That, to me, is what ethics under uncertainty looks like.

Not perfect knowledge.

Not vibes.

A disciplined way of keeping live harm visible without pretending to know more than you know.

This is where AI worries me.

Not because machines are uniquely bad at uncertainty. Humans are bad at it too. We rationalise. We freeze. We explain ourselves afterwards.

But AI can make partial sight look complete.

It can produce an answer in a confident shape. A score. A probability. A recommendation. Something that looks more finished than it is.

A human being sees the output and feels relief. Finally, something definite. Finally, the uncertainty has been turned into a number.

But a number is not certainty.

A probability is not an answer to the moral question.

It may be useful. It may be very useful. I am not arguing against measurement. I am arguing against letting measurement steal the uncertainty from the room.

Because the uncertainty is still there. It has only changed form.

If a system says there is a 73 percent risk, the moral problem has not vanished. Someone still has to ask what happens if the system is wrong, who bears that cost, how fast the harm could harden, and whether the person affected has any way to answer back.

That is where responsibility sits.

Not in pretending the tool is useless.

Not in obeying the tool as if it were fate.

In the gap between what the tool can see and what the situation still requires.

This is why I do not trust ethics that only works after the facts are settled.

A lot of moral judgement is easiest after the event. Once the outcome is known, everyone can tell a cleaner story. They can say the teacher should have reported sooner, or should not have interfered. They can say the doctor should have ordered the test, or should not have wasted resources. They can say the engineer should have known the system would fail, or should not have delayed deployment on weak evidence.

Afterwards, the world looks more obvious than it was.

That is hindsight doing its usual trick.

Mechanical Ethics has to work before that. It has to work in the fog, when the person acting does not yet know which future they are standing in.

That does not mean all decisions are equally defensible.

Uncertainty is not a moral solvent. It does not dissolve responsibility. It does not make every choice understandable and therefore acceptable.

The test is not only “did you know?”

The test is closer to: what did you do with the fact that you did not know?

Did you keep the situation observable? Did you preserve the chance to correct? Did you avoid forcing irreversible conclusions from weak signals? Did you protect against the worst plausible harm without turning suspicion into punishment? Did you allow the affected person, where possible, some way to answer?

Those are not easy demands.

But they are better than the two failures we keep falling into: waiting for certainty while harm hardens, or acting as if uncertainty gives us licence to do whatever power prefers.

This is also why “good intentions” are not enough.

The teacher may care. The doctor may care. The programmer may care. The official may care. I believe that, most of the time, people inside systems are not trying to harm anyone.

But care that cannot see its own uncertainty can still harm.

A person can be trying to help and still close a future too quickly. A system can be designed for safety and still erase someone’s ability to answer. A model can be built to reduce risk and still move risk onto the person least able to carry it.

That is not hypocrisy. It is structure.

And if it is structure, then moral language has to get more precise.

We need to stop treating uncertainty as a footnote.

Uncertainty is not the edge case. It is the normal condition.

Every decision is made from somewhere, with something missing. The ethical question is whether we build that missingness into the decision honestly, or hide it behind confidence.

I think this is one of the places where human ethics and machine ethics meet.

A human being has partial sight because they are embodied, limited, emotional, local. A machine has partial sight because it only has the data, training, architecture, tools, and context it has. These are not the same kind of limitation, but they rhyme enough to matter.

No bounded reasoner gets the whole world.

So an ethics for bounded reasoners cannot begin by demanding perfect knowledge.

It has to ask what kind of action remains decent, careful, and answerable when knowledge is incomplete.

For me, that is where Chapter 1 leads.

Something is here.

We do not see it fully.

We still have to choose.

The question is what responsibility demands while we are still partly blind.

My answer, at least at this stage, is this:

When you are uncertain, do not try to become certain by force.

Keep correction alive as a constraint on whatever you do next.

Do not close more future than you have to. Do not pretend the signal is stronger than it is. Do not wait so long that the harm you feared becomes permanent. Do not make the person inside the uncertainty carry the whole cost of your not knowing.

That is not a full ethics.

But it is a beginning.

And it is already more than “we didn’t know.”
