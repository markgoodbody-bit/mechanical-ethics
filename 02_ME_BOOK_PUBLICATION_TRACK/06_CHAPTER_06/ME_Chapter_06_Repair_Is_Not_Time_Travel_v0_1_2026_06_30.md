# Chapter 6 — Repair Is Not Time Travel

There is a kind of mistake that can be repaired.

A payment can be restored. A record can be corrected. A person can be reinstated. Money can be repaid. A bad decision can be reversed before it has taken root.

That kind of repair matters. I do not want to dismiss it.

But there is another kind of harm where repair arrives carrying the wrong word.

It says repaired, when it means acknowledged.

It says resolved, when it means recorded.

It says corrected, when it means we now know what happened.

That difference matters.

Repair is not time travel.

This chapter has to take the case I have been avoiding: the case with no better design waiting offstage. No clever intermediate option. No third way I can point to and say, “the system should have built that.”

So take a hospital at night.

Not as a medical lesson. I am not pretending to know how triage should be done. Real hospitals have protocols, training, escalation routes, and people who understand this far better than I do. That is exactly why the case is hard.

The point is not that the people in it are careless.

The point is that they are not.

There are two patients who need transfer to a larger unit. Both are deteriorating. There is one critical-care transfer team available for the next few hours. Bad weather has grounded the helicopter. The second team is already out. The doctors call, escalate, check the protocol, ring the receiving hospital, argue through the facts, and make the best decision they can with the information they have.

They transfer one patient.

The other waits.

By morning, the patient who waited has died.

Now what does Mechanical Ethics do with that?

It can ask whether the clocks were seen. They were.

It can ask whether the affected people were erased. They were not.

It can ask whether there was a correction route. There was escalation. There was review. There were people awake at three in the morning trying to find capacity that did not exist.

It can ask whether the system had enough intermediate options. In this version of the case, no. Not because someone designed it badly in the moment. The spare capacity simply was not there.

It can ask whether someone should answer for what happened. Yes, in some sense. But answer does not have to mean blame.

And then the tool reaches the wall.

Because one person is still dead.

The family may be told the protocol was followed. That may be true.

They may be told the decision was reasonable. That may be true.

They may be told the hospital will review the case. That may be necessary.

None of that returns the person who waited.

This is the point where I do not want the book to become clever.

There are things a moral system can do here. It can keep the loss visible. It can refuse to hide behind the word “unavoidable” too quickly. It can ask whether staffing, funding, geography, management, or previous choices made the night worse than it had to be. It can make sure the family is not spoken to as if they are a problem to be handled. It can record the residue honestly.

But it cannot save the person afterwards.

And it cannot always prove that someone should have chosen differently.

That is the part I want to leave standing.

A lot of systems hate residue.

They want outcomes placed into boxes: justified, unjustified, error, no error, complaint upheld, complaint not upheld, compensation due, no compensation due. Those categories may be necessary. People need decisions. Organisations need records. Law needs thresholds.

But the categories do not exhaust what happened.

A death can be unavoidable and still leave a wound in the world.

A decision can be reasonable and still be terrible.

A process can work as designed and still reveal that the world it served was too thin, too stretched, or simply cruel in the way scarcity is cruel.

I do not know how to make that clean.

I do not think it should be made clean.

This is why repair is not enough as a moral word.

Repair suggests that something broken has been put back together. Sometimes that is true. A cracked pipe can be repaired. A wrong number in a database can be repaired. A mistaken payment can be repaired if the correction arrives before the missed rent, the debt spiral, the lost job, the shame, the exhaustion.

But after some events, what remains is not a broken object waiting to be fixed.

It is a changed world.

The family has a different life. The staff carry a different memory. The surviving patient may carry a debt they never asked for. The institution has a record. The next decision will happen under the shadow of this one.

Calling all of that repair is dishonest.

It is residue.

Residue is what remains after the formal correction has done everything it can do.

Sometimes residue is grief. Sometimes it is debt. Sometimes it is distrust. Sometimes it is knowledge nobody wanted. Sometimes it is the memory that the world can ask you to choose and still leave you dirty afterwards.

That last phrase sounds harsh, but I think it is true.

There are situations where every available action leaves a mark.

Mechanical Ethics should not pretend otherwise.

If it becomes a system for washing the dirt off the decision, it has failed.

The temptation is strong. Once you have a framework, you want it to redeem the case. You want to say: here were the clocks, here was the pathway, here was the answerable role, here was the least harmful action, therefore the decision is morally clean.

But “least harmful” is not the same as clean.

Least harmful may still be awful.

Necessary may still leave residue.

Correct may still not be whole.

The hospital case is not there to make doctors look bad. It is there because I need a case where the tool cannot win. If I only use cases where better design, faster correction, or clearer answerability would have solved the problem, then Mechanical Ethics becomes too pleased with itself. It starts to look like every tragedy is secretly a design failure waiting for my framework to expose it.

That is not true.

Some tragedies are made worse by systems.

Some are produced by systems.

Some are not solved by systems at all.

Scarcity can be real. Time can be real. Bodies can fail. Weather can close a route. Two people can need one thing at the same time, and no amount of moral seriousness can produce the second thing by wanting it.

That is the wall.

I do not have a clever answer to it.

The best I can say is that the wall changes what honesty requires.

If the loss could not be prevented, do not pretend that makes it nothing.

If the decision was justified, do not pretend that makes it painless.

If the review finds no fault, do not pretend that means there is no residue.

If the family asks why, do not answer only with procedure.

If the staff did the best they could, do not use them as human shields for a shortage the institution would rather not discuss.

That is not repair.

It is witness, record, and responsibility after the fact.

Those are smaller words than salvation.

They are also more honest.

This matters for AI because AI systems will be tempted to overcount repair. They will be able to log a correction, update a model, restore an account, reverse a flag, or issue compensation, and then mark the incident closed.

From the system’s point of view, the state has changed back.

From the human side, it may not have.

A person wrongly ranked down for a job cannot always recover the missed interview. A person incorrectly flagged cannot always recover the trust lost in the room. A family wrongly deprioritised cannot always recover the week during which everyone organised around the wrong answer.

Even when the correction is real, the path taken still happened.

That is why residue has to be recorded separately from repair.

A system that treats correction as erasure is lying to itself.

And if an AI is trained only on the final corrected state, it may learn the lie very well. It may learn that the account was restored, the case was reopened, the payment was made, the appeal succeeded. It may not see the nights in between.

Those nights are part of the moral record.

Mechanical Ethics cannot make every harmed future whole again. That is a limit, not a feature. I do not want to turn it into a virtue. A limit is a limit.

What the framework can do is insist that the limit be named.

It can say: do not call loss repaired because your process has ended. Do not call residue emotion and then exclude it from the accounting. Do not make the people who suffered the delay prove again that the delay mattered.

But even that does not fix the thing.

It only stops the second wrong: the erasure of the first wrong by clean administrative language.

This is probably the most uncomfortable place in the book so far.

Not because the answer is complicated, but because there may not be an answer of the kind I keep wanting.

The person who died is not restored.

The family is not made whole.

The staff are not untouched.

The review may be fair, and still insufficient.

That is where repair ends.

What remains has to be carried.
