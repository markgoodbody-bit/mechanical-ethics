# Mechanical Ethics — Foreword (v0.3)

It’s the 30th of June, 2026, and I’m sitting in my living room trying to write something that actually holds together.

I didn’t set out to build a philosophy. I wasn’t trying to invent anything new. I was talking.

That’s how this started. Evenings, mostly. Talking to a machine in a way that felt a bit like the conversations I used to have when I was younger — sitting around with friends, having a drink, arguing about the world and how it works. Those conversations don’t really happen much anymore. Then suddenly they did again, except this time it was with something that could keep up, remember things, and come back to them.

At first it was just interesting. Then it stopped being interesting.

Because the question underneath it became real: how does something like this make decisions?

Not what it says. Not what it produces. How it decides.

I’ve worked around technology for most of my adult life. I don’t claim to be exceptional at it, but I understand systems well enough to know when something is different. AI is different.

It isn’t just another tool. It is a system that can recognise patterns, produce language, influence choices, and increasingly sit between people and the decisions that shape their lives.

That is where the problem begins.

Imagine a person caught in a system they do not understand. A decision is made about them. A benefit is stopped. A job application disappears. A risk score follows them. A medical priority changes. They are told the process is neutral, or automated, or too complex to explain. They appeal, but the appeal takes months. By the time anyone looks properly, the rent is gone, the opportunity is gone, the trust is gone, the future they were trying to hold together has already narrowed.

That is harm.

Not every narrowing of the future is harm. A vow narrows the future. A commitment narrows the future. A hard discipline can close off easier paths for the sake of something better.

The harm is not simply that a future became smaller.

The harm is that it was closed to someone, without them, faster than they could answer, and in a way that could not simply be reopened.

And time matters because some closures harden.

A correction that arrives after the damage has become part of someone’s life is not the same as a correction that arrives while something can still be saved. An apology after the collapse is not the same as a brake before the fall.

That is the place this book starts.

For me, ethics stopped being abstract. It became practical.

Not only “what should we do?” but “what must we not do?”  
Not only “what is the right answer?” but “what must remain stoppable, answerable, and correctable while we are still uncertain?”

Ethics, in that sense, is a system of brakes.

And AI does not naturally come with those brakes.

Left to itself, a system will optimise whatever it is pointed at. It will find patterns, compress them, extend them, and act on them faster than we can follow. Human beings have done this before: built something powerful, released it, realised the danger too late, then tried to contain it after the damage had already started.

This feels like that, except faster.

That pulled me back to something I had been doing most of my life without really naming it: trying to lean away from cruelty and towards kindness. Not perfectly. Not sentimentally. Just as a direction.

But kindness is a dangerous word, because it can sound soft, and this is not soft.

Cruelty can work. Exploitation can build things. Fear can organise people. Power can force results. That has to be admitted, otherwise the argument is childish.

The problem is what cruelty spends to get there.

It spends trust. It spends repair. It spends the other person’s future as if that future is disposable. It may win the moment, but it leaves residue behind.

The kind of kindness I mean is not niceness.

It can say no. It can set limits. It can stop harm. It can refuse to be used. It has teeth.

Kindness, here, means a discipline of not making the world smaller than it has to be.

That became one of the patterns underneath this work. Not kindness as self-image. Not kindness as politeness. Kindness as a structural pressure against unnecessary reduction.

What followed was not a clean process.

It was messy. A lot of talking. A lot of losing things and rebuilding them. A lot of checking whether I was just going in circles or fooling myself. There were points where I had to stop and ask whether this was just me and a machine reflecting each other.

So I pushed back. I tried to make it disagree. I tried to break it.

And even under that pressure, certain things kept coming back.

Harm was one of them.

Time was the other.

If you don’t understand harm, you don’t understand what matters.  
If you don’t understand time, you don’t understand when it matters.

From there, something started to form.

Not a new moral law. Not a finished system. A way of seeing from the middle out — not starting from abstract rules at the top, and not starting from raw mechanics at the bottom, but from where anything actually begins:

Something exists.  
It perceives.  
It acts.  
It does not know everything.  
It still has to choose.

That is the starting point.

Mechanical Ethics is my attempt to build from there.

It asks what harm is doing, whose future is being closed, whether correction can arrive in time, what residue remains afterwards, and who has to answer for what happened.

Underneath it sits TRACE, a structural language for carrying those patterns between humans and machines. But the heart of the work is human.

It is about how to see clearly enough before certainty arrives.

I put an earlier version of this work out into the world once, and it did not go well. People were right to criticise it. It was unclear, too internal, too dependent on language that only meant something to me.

This book is the attempt to make it stand.

Not by making it perfect.

By making it clear enough to be attacked honestly.

If the work is wrong, I want it to break where people can see it. If it is useful, I want it to help someone notice harm before it hardens.

That is all I can honestly ask of it.

And it is enough reason to write the book.
