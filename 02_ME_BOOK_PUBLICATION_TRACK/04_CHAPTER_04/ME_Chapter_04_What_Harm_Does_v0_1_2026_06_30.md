# Chapter 4 — What Harm Does

The easiest version of harm is the one I have already used.

Someone is caught in a system. Their future narrows without them. The clock runs faster than the appeal. By the time anyone corrects the decision, some part of their life has already closed.

That is real harm, and I do not want to weaken it.

But if I leave harm there, the whole book becomes too easy.

There is a harder case.

A person chooses the narrowing.

Not because someone has a gun to their head. Not because the system has trapped them in some obvious way. They choose. Calmly enough. Freely enough, at least from the outside.

A man has a decent job. Not his dream, but decent. He is tired of it. He wants to open a cafe. He has talked about it for years. He has a notebook full of ideas: the coffee, the music, the chairs, the way the place will feel in winter. His friends tell him to be careful. His parents tell him not to risk the savings. He knows all that.

He does it anyway.

He signs the lease. He leaves the job. He spends the money. For a while it feels alive.

Then it goes wrong.

The rent is too high. The footfall is lower than he thought. He works every hour. He stops seeing people. Six months later, he is exhausted, in debt, and trying to work out whether he can go back to the kind of life he left.

His future has narrowed.

So is that harm?

This is where Mechanical Ethics strains.

If I say yes too quickly, the framework becomes paternalistic. It starts treating any bad outcome from a risky choice as if the person was harmed by the choice itself. That is not right. Human beings have to be allowed to risk their futures. We have to be allowed to commit, sacrifice, fail, and choose paths other people think are unwise.

A life where no one is allowed to narrow their own future is not a protected life.

It is a managed one.

That matters. A vow narrows the future. Parenthood narrows the future. Art narrows the future. Training for something difficult narrows the future. Love narrows the future. So does saying no to a life that is safe but deadening.

If harm simply means “fewer options than before,” then almost every serious commitment becomes harm.

That cannot be right.

But if I say no too quickly, the opposite problem appears.

Some chosen narrowings really are damaging. People freely choose things that destroy them. They choose the addiction again. They choose the manipulative partner again. They choose the contract that looks like freedom and becomes a trap. They choose the dream because everyone selling the dream made the risks sound smaller than they were.

From the outside, it may look chosen. Inside it, the choice may be full of pressure, fantasy, shame, hope, exhaustion, or hunger for a different life.

So I cannot escape by saying: chosen means clean.

It does not.

This is the problem.

Mechanical Ethics wants to protect future-space, but agency itself includes the right to spend future-space. Sometimes the most human thing a person can do is close off easier futures for the sake of a harder one.

That means an open future is not automatically the good.

This is one of the places where the tool has to admit a limit.

It can notice reduction. It can notice hardening. It can ask whether a future can still be reopened. It can ask who chose, under what pressure, with what information, and who benefited from the narrowing.

But it cannot stand outside a life and simply count options.

A person is not a portfolio of possible futures to be maximised.

That sentence matters to me, because without it this whole project becomes colder than I want it to be. It becomes another machine for managing people in the name of protecting them.

So what does harm do, if it is not just option-loss?

I think harm damages the field in which a person or affected thing can continue meaningfully.

That is less neat than “harm closes futures,” but it is closer.

A chosen commitment can narrow options while deepening a life. A destructive trap can leave many options on paper while making the person less able to reach any of them. The question is not just how many futures remain. It is what has happened to the person’s real capacity to move, answer, repair, relate, and continue.

In the cafe case, the answer is not obvious.

Maybe he was harmed.

Maybe he was not.

Maybe he failed, and failure is part of agency. Maybe the debt will take years from him in a way that really does damage his future. Maybe the attempt gives him something he would not trade away, even if it cost him. Maybe all of those are true at once.

This is the kind of case where I do not want the framework to pretend to be cleaner than life.

The best it can do is ask sharper questions.

Was the risk visible to him? Not perfectly visible, because nothing is. Visible enough.

Could he answer back to the people shaping the risk? The landlord. The lender. The franchise adviser. The friend encouraging him. The glossy small-business story in his own head.

Was there a way out, or did the decision become a trap as soon as he signed?

Who benefited from his hope?

What residue remains after the failure? Debt, shame, knowledge, grief, pride, skill, bitterness, a better sense of himself. Those are not the same kind of residue. They cannot be added up like numbers.

That is why I say harm is not just pain.

Pain may be part of growth. Loss may be part of love. Discipline may hurt and still be chosen for a good reason. Even regret does not prove harm, because regret can belong to a real life.

Harm is different.

Harm is the kind of damage that reduces the being’s capacity to continue as itself, or makes the next honest movement of life less reachable.

That can happen through force. It can happen through deception. It can happen through delay. It can happen through abandonment. It can also happen through choices that look free but are bent by pressures the person could not properly see.

But I need to be careful here.

If I stretch “pressure” too far, freedom disappears. Every choice has pressure in it. Money is pressure. Family is pressure. Desire is pressure. Fear is pressure. Hope is pressure. Being alive is pressure.

So the question cannot be: was there any pressure?

The question has to be: did the pressure distort the person’s ability to choose in a way that someone else used, ignored, or intensified?

That is a more useful question.

It keeps the cafe owner from becoming a helpless victim just because he failed. It also keeps the landlord, lender, platform, adviser, or system from washing their hands by saying “he chose it.”

Choice matters.

But choice is not magic.

A signature does not turn every future closure clean. Consent does not erase structure. And failure does not automatically prove someone was harmed.

That is the narrow path.

I do not think Mechanical Ethics fully solves it.

I think it marks the place where we should stop lying.

We lie when we call every chosen loss freedom.

We also lie when we call every painful choice harm.

The truth is harder. People are allowed to spend their futures. They are also vulnerable to having that spending shaped, harvested, or made irreversible by others.

A decent ethics has to protect both things at once: the right to choose real risk, and the duty not to turn another person’s risk into a trap.

That is not an elegant rule.

It is a tension.

And it should stay a tension.

Because if we resolve it too quickly, we lose something important. Resolve it one way, and we become paternalists. Resolve it the other, and we become apologists for exploitation.

So when this book says harm closes futures, that is still true, but it is not enough.

Harm closes, distorts, or hardens futures in a way that damages meaningful continuation.

That phrase is not as clean as the first version. I know that.

But sometimes the less clean sentence is the more honest one.

This also matters for AI.

An AI system will be very good at counting options if we train it to count options. It may be able to say that a person technically has choices: click here, appeal there, accept this, reject that, opt out, find another job, take another route.

On paper, the future may look open.

In life, it may not be.

That is one danger.

The other danger is the opposite: a system built to preserve options could become suffocating. It might stop risky choices, flag commitments as dangerous, smooth away difficult paths, and call that protection.

It could become a parent that never lets the child climb.

That is not care.

Care does not mean keeping every future open forever.

No one can live like that.

Care means paying attention to whether the narrowing is authored, answerable, and still connected to the person’s continuation, rather than imposed, hidden, harvested, or hardened beyond repair.

That is the best I can make it for now.

Not a perfect definition.

A warning against two false comforts.

The first says: if they chose it, there is no harm.

The second says: if it hurt them, someone must stop them choosing it.

Both are wrong.

Between them is the difficult human space where agency and harm overlap.

That is where Mechanical Ethics has to work.

And that is where it is weakest.
