# Chapter 1 — Something Is Here

I think the mistake is that we usually start too far away.

We start with the theory. Or the rule. Or the outcome. Or the policy. Or the machine. We stand above the situation and try to solve it from there.

I understand why. It feels safer. If you start with a principle, you have something clean to hold. If you start with a system, you can draw boxes and arrows. If you start with code, you can pretend the moral problem is somewhere else.

But that is not how it feels to be alive inside a decision.

You do not wake up as a principle.

You wake up as someone.

You wake up tired, or worried, or late for work. You remember the thing you forgot yesterday. You look at your phone. There is a message you do not want to answer. Something is already happening before you have had time to think properly.

That is closer to where ethics starts.

Not outside life, looking down at it. Inside it.

Something is here.

I know that sounds almost stupidly simple, but I do not think it is. I think a lot of harm starts because that simple thing gets skipped.

Take a normal sort of case. Nothing dramatic. No science fiction. No killer robot. Just a letter.

A woman gets a letter saying her payment has stopped. Maybe it is a benefit. Maybe it is support for housing. Maybe it is something she depends on but does not fully control. The letter has a reference number on it. It explains the decision in a way that sounds official but not human. It says she can appeal.

So she appeals.

Now, from the system's point of view, something has happened. A decision was made. A route exists. There is a process. There is a timescale. Somewhere there is a record saying that the case is under review.

But from her side, the rent is due Friday.

That is the part systems keep missing.

The system has a process. She has a clock.

Not because rules do not matter, and not because systems do not matter, but because the moral problem is not fully visible from either end.

From the top, it may look like policy.

From the bottom, it may look like data.

In the middle, it is someone trying to keep a life from narrowing before anyone with power has properly looked.

That is the place Mechanical Ethics begins.

Before we ask what category she fits, or whether the system followed procedure, or whether the model was statistically accurate, we have to notice that there is an affected centre in the field. A person for whom this is not an output. A person living through the time the system treats as delay.

That does not settle the answer. It does not mean she is automatically right. It does not mean every decision against her is harm. It does not mean feeling bad is the same as being wronged.

But it does mean the question has started in the right place.

Something is here, and what happens next can matter to it.

That sounds obvious. It is not obvious in practice.

In practice, people disappear inside language. They become cases, scores, claimants. Some of those words are necessary. I have worked in systems long enough to know that you need categories. You cannot run anything at scale if every single person has to be understood from scratch every time.

But categories are dangerous when they start pretending they are the person.

That is one of the first things I want to hold onto.

A score is not a life. A form can tell you something, but it is not the person.

And that matters even more with AI, because AI is very good at treating patterns as if they are the world.

A model does not have to hate anyone to harm them. It does not have to be cruel in the human sense. It can simply take the information it has, make a prediction, pass that prediction into a process, and then the process moves.

The person on the other side might never know where the decision really came from.

They might not know what to challenge. They might not know who to speak to. They might not know whether anyone has actually seen them at all.

That is not a small detail. That is the moral situation.

So when I say Mechanical Ethics starts in the middle, I mean it starts there: with the affected thing that can be missed by both theory and mechanism.

It starts with the being inside the consequences.

Then it asks what can be seen.

No one sees everything. That is not just a human weakness. It is the condition of any bounded intelligence. A person sees from inside their own life. A government department sees through its rules and records. A company sees through targets. An AI sees through the data and structure it has been given.

None of that is the whole world.

That should make us cautious, but it should not paralyse us. We still have to act. The woman still needs an answer. The rent is still due. The clock does not stop because the information is incomplete.

This is the uncomfortable bit.

Ethics cannot wait for perfect sight.

But it also cannot let partial sight pretend to be certainty.

So the first question is not, “What is the perfect answer?” It is something more ordinary and more difficult:

What are we doing to this person while we are still unsure?

That question changes the shape of the whole thing.

It does not let the system hide behind procedure. It does not let the person making the decision pretend delay is neutral. It does not let the machine's output become reality just because it arrived in a confident format.

And it does not let us clean everything up afterwards by saying the right route existed on paper.

A route that arrives too late is not the same as a route that works.

That is why time enters so early.

If the appeal comes through after she has lost the flat, the correction is real, but it is not the same. If the payment is restored after the debt has spiralled, that matters, but it is not time travel. If someone eventually says the decision was wrong, that may be necessary, but it does not put the same future back in her hands.

This is not about blaming one person behind a desk. Most of the time there is no single villain. That is part of the problem. Harm often travels through systems precisely because no one person feels like they are doing the whole thing.

One person designs the rule. Another maintains the software. Another answers the phone. Another signs off the appeal. Another audits the statistics six months later and says the overall error rate was acceptable.

Meanwhile, the person inside the decision lived every day of the delay.

That is why I do not want ethics to begin with blame.

Blame can come later, if it belongs. Responsibility can come later. Law can come later. But the first thing is to see the harm while it is still live.

This is also where the question of AI has to be handled carefully.

An AI system is not a person just because it speaks like one. I do not trust that shortcut. Language is not enough. Fluency is not enough. A system saying “I understand” is not proof that anything like human understanding is happening inside it.

But I also do not trust the opposite shortcut: “it is only a machine, so there is nothing to consider.”

That may be true in many cases. It may be true in nearly all cases now. But if the whole point of this age is that artificial systems are becoming stranger, more capable, and more entangled with human decisions, then we should not build an ethics that can only recognise what already looks familiar.

We need a starting point thin enough to ask the question without pretending we already know the answer.

That is what “something is here” is trying to do.

Not “this is a person.”

Not “this has rights.”

Not “this must be protected in the same way as a human being.”

Just: do not erase the affected centre before you have looked.

That applies downward, to the person inside the decision. It may one day apply outward, to forms of intelligence we do not yet understand. And it applies sideways, to animals, places, communities, and other futures that can be damaged because they were never granted enough attention to enter the room.

I know that is broad. It has to be handled carefully. A book can make this too wide and end up saying nothing. But the opposite mistake is worse right now. The opposite mistake is to let our categories do the seeing for us.

This is the first movement, then.

Not a law. Not a proof. Not a complete system.

A way of beginning.

Before the rule, before the score, before the model, before the explanation after the fact, ask what is here, what can be affected, what clock is running, and what cannot simply be restored if we are wrong.

That is where the rest of this book builds from.
