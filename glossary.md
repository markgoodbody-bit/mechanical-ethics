# Glossary

This glossary defines terms used in the Correction Window Test.

The terms are working language for a public diagnostic. They are not claims of a complete theory.

## Affected person

The person or group whose practical options, burden, standing, access, safety, money, time, care, or future changes because of a system's selection.

## After-the-fact review

A review route that can expose failure, correct records, compensate, sanction, or prevent recurrence, but usually cannot change the original consequence before it hardens.

## Carrier

The real mechanism that makes a safeguard capable of action.

A carrier may include authority, resources, pause power, reversal power, deadlines, evidence access, compensation authority, technical rollback, enforcement, or external review.

A policy statement without a carrier is weak protection.

## Correction

An action that can stop, pause, reverse, repair, compensate, or prevent recurrence after an error or harmful path is detected.

Correction is stronger when it reaches the affected person before hardening.

## Correction window

The time between the first possible error or harmful selection and the point where the consequence becomes difficult, costly, or impossible to reverse.

## Evidence

The record that lets someone show what happened.

Useful evidence may include inputs, outputs, reasons, timestamps, notices, challenge routes, human-review records, audit trails, override logs, and repair outcomes.

## False safeguard

A safeguard that exists in wording, policy, dashboard, audit, form, or public claim but cannot meaningfully alter the affected person's path in time.

## Hardening

The point where ordinary correction no longer restores the original path.

Hardening can be legal, practical, financial, social, technical, clinical, informational, or institutional.

Examples:

- job filled;
- benefit stopped;
- rent missed;
- medical window closed;
- account closed;
- model output propagated;
- data copied downstream;
- evidence deleted;
- debt recovered;
- deadline passed.

## Human oversight

A human involvement claim.

Human oversight is not automatically protection. It matters only if the human has time, evidence, authority, incentives, and permission to intervene before hardening.

## Live protection

A safeguard that can still change the affected person's path before the relevant consequence hardens.

## Partial protection

A safeguard that has some real protective features, but one or more of timing, authority, evidence, access, standing, or resources is incomplete.

## Practical irreversibility

A state where reversal may still be theoretically possible but is no longer realistically available to the affected person at tolerable cost.

## Selection

The point where a system chooses, ranks, recommends, approves, refuses, classifies, flags, routes, triggers, or changes something that affects the world.

A selection may be advisory on paper but practically binding if downstream actors normally follow it.

## Standing

The ability to trigger challenge, review, protection, or correction.

A person may carry the consequence without having standing to challenge the route that affected them.

## Unknown

A valid result where public evidence does not show enough to classify the safeguard.

Unknown should not be converted into safety, failure, or reassurance.

## Working result label

A judgement prompt used by the test.

The labels are not validated instrument categories. They are a way to state the result clearly while preserving uncertainty.
