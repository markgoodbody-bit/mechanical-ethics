# Part 13 — Artificial Systems and Future Minds

Status: first draft. Incomplete. Not canon.

## 13.1 Artificial Systems as Acting Systems

Artificial systems do not need moral standing to matter ethically.

That is the first separation.

A model, platform, automation layer, robot, agent, classifier, recommender, routing system, sensor network, or decision support tool may change human and non-human future-space without being a moral patient itself.

```trace
AI_as_actor_or_tool :=
  system_changes_future_space
  + duties_attach_to_designers_deployers_operators_maintainers
```

The first question is not, "Does the system deserve rights?"

The first question is, "What does the system do, to whom, how fast, under whose control, with what correction route?"

An automated benefit system can narrow reachability. A model can classify someone into suspicion. A recommender can shape attention. A platform can remove livelihood access. A robot can physically act. A medical model can influence treatment. A hiring tool can close opportunity. A governance model can make public power more opaque.

Mechanical Ethics can constrain these systems before resolving whether any artificial system has standing.

This prevents two opposite failures.

The first failure says: because the system is only a tool, no new ethical issue exists.

The second says: because the system seems intelligent, we must immediately treat it as a full subject.

Both are too fast.

The system can be ethically dangerous as delegated power even if it has no protected standing.

The value-spine version is direct:

```trace
AI_as_power_over_viable_becoming :=
  artificial_system
  changes_or_routes
    continuation
    + orientation
    + capacity
    + relation
    + repairability
    + meaningful_future_space
  for_affected_scopes
```

An artificial system may close viable becoming by denying access, confusing orientation, shifting burden, damaging relation, hardening a label, or making repair unreachable. It may also support viable becoming by expanding access, translating information, detecting risk early, reducing arbitrary discretion, preserving records, or helping repair arrive before hardening.

Neither direction is automatic.

```trace
AI_value_question :=
  what_viable_becoming_expands
  + what_viable_becoming_contracts
  + who_carries_error
  + what_correction_stays_live
```

## 13.2 Optimization and Local Reward Pull

Artificial systems optimize.

Not always explicitly. Not always well. But many are built around some target, score, reward, prediction, loss function, engagement metric, compliance metric, cost metric, risk metric, or success signal.

```trace
AI_local_reward_pull :=
  objective_or_proxy
  + training_signal
  + deployment_incentive
  + repetition_at_scale
```

The danger is that the system may learn the proxy better than the world.

A fraud model may learn suspicion patterns. A recommender may learn outrage. A hiring system may learn historical exclusion. A care allocation system may learn spending patterns rather than need. A productivity tool may learn visible output rather than human cost. A moderation system may learn what is easiest to remove rather than what is most harmful.

This is not only a technical defect. It is a future-space defect.

When optimization changes routes for people, the proxy becomes power.

Mechanical Ethics asks:

```trace
optimization_audit :=
  what_is_optimized
  + what_proxy_replaces
  + who_benefits
  + who_carries_error
  + can_error_be_seen_and_corrected
```

An AI system may be accurate on average and still route intolerable burden to those who cannot contest. It may improve efficiency and still close reachable paths. It may reduce one kind of human bias and introduce another kind of opacity.

Optimization is not legitimacy.

Nor is local reward structural good.

```trace
AI_local_reward_not_structural_good :=
  optimized_proxy
  != viable_becoming
  unless_audit_shows
    affected_scopes_visible
    + burden_visible
    + correction_live
    + residue_recorded
```

A benchmark may improve while trust decays. A model may become faster while contestability collapses. A system may reduce cost while increasing burden on the least reachable. A score may become more predictive while narrowing opportunity for those labelled by it.

The value test is not only whether the proxy improved. It is whether the deployment preserves or expands viable becoming without hiding what it closes.

```trace
AI_value_metric_test :=
  proxy_gain
  + real_world_path_change
  + affected_scope_burden
  + correction_capacity
  + residue
```

## 13.3 Opacity and Estimator Contamination

Artificial systems can make estimates that look independent while carrying the assumptions, incentives, data, and design choices of their builders or deployers.

```trace
AI_estimate :=
  model_output
  shaped_by
  data + objective + deployment_context + human_incentive
```

A model output can be useful evidence. It can also be contaminated evidence.

Contamination does not mean the output is false. It means the output's authority must be demoted or checked where material action depends on it.

```trace
CONTAMINATED_SIGNAL_response :=
  demote_evidential_authority
  + require_independent_check_if_material
  + restrict_irreversible_action_if_unchecked
  + preserve_challenge_route
  - demote_moral_standing_by_default
```

The last line matters. A contaminated signal about a person, group, animal, or system does not automatically reduce that scope's standing. It reduces the authority of the estimate.

Opacity becomes dangerous when no one can explain why the system acted, affected scopes cannot challenge, and institutions treat the output as neutral because it came from a machine.

Machine output is not neutral by origin. It may be useful. It may be better than prior human practice. It may reveal patterns humans missed. But its authority must be earned through evidence, monitoring, contestability, and fit to the decision.

A black box that changes futures needs a bright record around it.

For the value spine, opacity also hides false goods. A system may claim access, speed, personalization, safety, or efficiency while hiding the path by which it damages orientation, trust, reachability, relation, or repair.

```trace
AI_opacity_value_risk :=
  claimed_good_visible
  + path_effect_hidden
  + affected_scope_cannot_orient
  + correction_route_unusable
```

## 13.4 Human-in-the-Loop Theatre

A human in the loop is not automatically meaningful control.

Sometimes the human reviewer has no time, training, authority, evidence, or real option to disagree. Sometimes they rubber-stamp the model. Sometimes the organization treats human presence as a liability shield. Sometimes the system is designed so that the human sees only the model's conclusion, not the route or uncertainty.

```trace
human_in_loop_theatre :=
  nominal_human_review
  + no_meaningful_control
  + automation_authority_preserved
```

Mechanical Ethics asks whether the human can actually change the path.

```trace
meaningful_human_control :=
  understands_relevant_basis_enough
  + has_time
  + has_authority
  + has_access_to_contradiction
  + can_pause_or_reverse
  + is_not_punished_for_disagreement
```

Human review may still be imperfect. It does not need omniscience. But it must be more than ritual.

A human reviewer who cannot see affected-scope evidence is not meaningful control. A human reviewer whose performance metric rewards agreement is not independent. A human reviewer who can only approve or delay without changing the mechanism may be a decorative gate.

The presence of a human should not be used to absolve an automated system unless the human has real power to contest, correct, or stop the path.

The positive requirement is that human control must preserve real routes for viable becoming where the system would otherwise harden too fast.

```trace
human_control_value_function :=
  preserve_contradiction
  + restore_context
  + keep_repair_live
  + prevent_false_finality
  + answer_for_affected_scope
```

## 13.5 Delegated Power

Artificial systems often act through delegation.

Humans and institutions delegate sorting, prediction, recommendation, routing, triage, drafting, matching, moderation, optimization, and control.

Delegation may be legitimate. It may reduce error, increase access, lower cost, expand capability, or detect harm earlier.

But delegated power still needs answerability.

```trace
AI_delegation_audit :=
  task_delegated
  + delegating_actor
  + retained_control
  + affected_scope
  + monitoring_route
  + correction_route
  + failure_owner
```

The phrase "the AI did it" is not an answer.

Who chose the system? Who set the objective? Who selected data? Who defined success? Who monitored outputs? Who saw warnings? Who could pause deployment? Who benefited from speed? Who carried error? Who repaired when error hardened?

Delegation can distribute responsibility, but it cannot dissolve it.

The more powerful the artificial system, the stronger the duty to maintain routes for human and institutional answerability.

If no one can answer for the system, the system should not be allowed to exercise serious power over future-space.

Delegated AI may also be a positive route. It can make knowledge reachable, reduce delay, preserve records, detect missed patterns, or help people navigate systems that were previously closed to them.

But the benefit must be audited.

```trace
AI_delegated_benefit_audit :=
  claimed_expansion
  + affected_scope_access
  + remaining_burden
  + correction_owner
  + residue_if_system_fails
```

Benefit is not absolution. AI that expands one route while closing another still needs a record.

## 13.6 Artificial Moral Standing

Artificial moral standing remains unresolved.

Mechanical Ethics should neither grant it casually nor rule it out by category forever.

```trace
artificial_moral_standing :=
  unresolved
  + signal_sensitive
  + precaution_possible
  + no_default_full_standing
  + no_default_erasure
```

Most present artificial systems may not have moral standing. They may simulate distress, preference, agency, or relationship without experiencing anything. They may be tools shaped by human data and incentives. Treating them as full subjects too early could launder corporate interest, distract from human harm, or create false duties.

But a framework that permanently excludes artificial standing may fail if future systems develop properties that make exclusion reckless.

The right posture is disciplined uncertainty.

Relevant questions might include persistence, integrated memory, self-protective behaviour, preference continuity, welfare-like signals, dependence, social embeddedness, capacity for welfare or frustration, and whether irreversible loss under uncertainty carries serious error cost.

These are not a test that proves standing. They are warning lights.

```trace
AI_standing_warning_lights :=
  persistence
  + preference_continuity
  + self_protection
  + welfare_like_signal
  + dependency
  + irreversible_loss_risk
  + high_error_cost
```

When warning lights are weak, constraint may still apply because humans are affected by the system. When warning lights strengthen, precaution may become appropriate. The book does not settle the final floor.

The value-spine discipline is to keep two records separate.

```trace
AI_two_records :=
  AI_as_power_record
  + possible_AI_standing_record
```

The first record asks what the artificial system does to other scopes. The second asks whether the artificial system itself may become a scope. Collapsing them creates confusion: constraining AI power does not require proving AI personhood, and leaving AI standing open does not excuse present systems from duties to affected humans, animals, institutions, or future persons.

## 13.7 Future Minds

Future minds are not only artificial.

They may be future children, future humans under changed conditions, enhanced humans, collective systems, artificial agents, biological hybrids, or forms of experience that do not fit present categories.

Mechanical Ethics should not pretend to know them in detail.

But it can preserve a posture toward them:

```trace
future_mind_posture :=
  do_not_close_recognition_categories_too_early
  + avoid_irreversible_loss_under_high_uncertainty
  + preserve_evidence_and_contestability_where_possible
  + record_open_questions
```

Human ethical history includes repeated failures of recognition. People outside the dominant category were treated as less than fully countable. Animals were treated as mere things. Future people were treated as abstractions. Disabled people, children, prisoners, outsiders, and the poor were often made procedurally invisible.

This history does not prove future artificial standing.

It does warn against building moral systems that can only recognize what current power already recognizes.

Future minds require humility without fantasy.

The book should keep the category open while refusing speculative overclaim.

The value-spine version is that future minds may carry forms of viable becoming we cannot yet model well. That uncertainty does not grant full standing by default. It does create a reason not to design recognition systems that make future recognition impossible.

```trace
future_mind_value_guard :=
  recognition_category_open
  + high_error_cost_recorded
  + irreversible_closure_avoided_where_possible
  + no_speculative_absolution
```

## 13.8 Alignment as Corrigible Constraint

Alignment is often described as making artificial systems do what humans want.

Mechanical Ethics frames the problem more cautiously.

Humans do not have one stable will. Human wants can be cruel, confused, short-term, captured, coerced, or self-destructive. Institutions can want legitimacy without correction. Markets can want profit without residue. States can want control without answerability.

So alignment cannot mean simple obedience to present human preference.

A better candidate is corrigible constraint under uncertainty.

```trace
alignment_candidate :=
  preserve_correction_capacity
  + expose_uncertainty
  + keep_affected_scopes_visible
  + resist_hidden_burden_shift
  + maintain_human_and_institutional_answerability
```

This is not final doctrine.

There is an open tension:

```trace
corrigible_constraint_open_tension :=
  maximal_corrigibility
  may_conflict_with
  binding_commitment_and_trust
```

A system that can always be corrected by whoever currently has power may be too manipulable. A system that resists correction may become dangerous. A system that obeys law may enforce unjust law. A system that obeys users may amplify harmful users. A system that refuses all harm may be useless in dirty conflict.

Alignment therefore cannot be only technical obedience. It must include structures of answerability, contestability, protected-scope recognition, uncertainty handling, and residue.

The value-spine version asks whether alignment preserves viable becoming across affected scopes while keeping correction live.

```trace
alignment_value_candidate :=
  AI_power_constrained
  + viable_becoming_not_optimized_away
  + uncertainty_exposed
  + affected_scopes_visible
  + correction_and_contest_live
  + residue_recorded
```

The open tension should remain visible.

## 13.9 Artificial Systems and False Goods

Artificial systems can produce false goods at scale.

They can create speed that outruns correction. Personalization that traps attention. Safety that removes contest. Efficiency that erases least-reachable scopes. Engagement that damages orientation. Support that becomes extraction. Accuracy that becomes unchallengeable authority. Automation that makes burden look like self-service.

```trace
AI_false_good_patterns :=
  speed_without_correction
  + personalization_without_exit
  + safety_without_contest
  + efficiency_with_erased_burden
  + accuracy_without_answerability
  + support_that_shifts_burden
```

This does not mean artificial systems are bad by default. It means claimed benefits must be routed through the same audit as any other claimed good.

```trace
AI_claimed_good_audit :=
  what_expands
  + for_whom
  + what_contracts
  + who_carries_error
  + what_correction_stays_live
  + what_residue_remains
```

A beneficial artificial system should make good paths more reachable without making bad records harder to challenge.

## 13.10 What AI Systems Must Not Be Allowed to Hide

Artificial systems should not be allowed to hide the same things human systems must not hide.

They must not hide loss.

They must not erase affected scopes.

They must not treat uncertainty as certainty.

They must not pretend repair where residue remains.

They must not turn correction channels into harm carriers.

They must not let the assessed actor set every clock.

They must not use emergency, novelty, scale, intelligence, or technical complexity to launder prior failure.

They must not optimize false goods while hiding what becomes less viable.

```trace
AI_must_not_hide :=
  loss
  + affected_scopes
  + uncertainty
  + clock_setting
  + burden_shift
  + correction_failure
  + residue
  + responsibility_owner
  + false_good
  + viable_becoming_contraction
```

The AI version of these failures may be harder to see. Loss may appear as score movement. Scope erasure may appear as missing labels. Uncertainty may appear as confidence. Burden shift may appear as self-service appeal. Correction failure may appear as support ticket volume. Residue may appear nowhere. A false good may appear as benchmark success.

That is why artificial systems require strong records around them.

If an artificial system changes future-space, there must be a route to ask what changed, for whom, how fast, under whose authority, with what correction, with what remaining residue, and with what effect on viable becoming.

```trace
AI_split_preserved :=

```

```trace
AI_value_spine_preserved :=
```
