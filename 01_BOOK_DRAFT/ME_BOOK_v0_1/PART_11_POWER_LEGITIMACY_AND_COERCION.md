# Part 11 — Power, Legitimacy, and Coercion

Status: first draft. Incomplete. Not canon.

Warning: this part frames more than it solves. It contains high-risk value-cruxes: legitimacy, coercion, punishment, desert, dignity, force, and acceptable sacrifice. These must not be treated as settled because the book has reached them.

## 11.1 Power That Changes Futures

Power is the capacity to change another scope's future-space.

That definition is deliberately mechanical. It does not yet say whether the power is lawful, legitimate, wise, abusive, necessary, or kind.

```trace
power :=
  capacity_to_change
  another_scope_future_space
```

Power may be physical, legal, economic, informational, social, technical, administrative, military, parental, medical, educational, algorithmic, or institutional. It may act quickly or slowly. It may act openly or invisibly. It may act through force, rules, defaults, incentives, classifications, queues, permissions, records, or delays.

A system has power when it can open or close paths for others.

A person with a weapon has power. A state with police power has power. A landlord has power over housing. A platform has power over reach. A school has power over a child's record. A hospital has power over access to care. A model has power when its outputs become routes. A bureaucracy has power when its timing decides whether correction remains meaningful.

Mechanical Ethics does not begin by saying all power is bad.

Without power, no one could protect, repair, coordinate, teach, build, rescue, govern, or care. The question is not whether power exists. The question is whether power remains answerable to the future-space it changes.

```trace
power_question :=
  whose_future_space_can_be_changed
  + by_whom
  + through_what_mechanism
  + under_what_constraint
  + with_what_correction_route
```

The value-spine version is this:

```trace
power_over_viable_becoming :=
  capacity_to_shape
    continuation
    + orientation
    + capacity
    + relation
    + repairability
    + meaningful_future_space
  for_another_scope
```

This is why power is ethically dense. It does not merely move resources or enforce rules. It can shape what another scope can become. It can preserve a route, open a path, teach, protect, heal, coordinate, and repair. It can also trap, confuse, isolate, humiliate, burden, silence, or close a path before the affected scope can answer.

```trace
power_can_support :=
  protection
  + coordination
  + repair
  + learning
  + care
  + trust_infrastructure
  + shared_future_space

power_can_damage :=
  closure
  + dependency_capture
  + false_finality
  + unreachable_contest
  + trust_damage
  + unrepairable_burden
```

Power that cannot answer becomes domination.

## 11.2 Legitimacy as Answerable Authority

Legitimacy is one of the hardest words in this book.

It is tempting to define legitimacy as lawful authority. That is too narrow. Some lawful authority is abusive. Some morally necessary action may occur before law has caught up. It is also tempting to define legitimacy as good outcome. That is too loose. Good outcomes can be used to excuse unanswerable power.

Mechanical Ethics should not pretend to solve legitimacy completely here.

It can frame a candidate structure:

```trace
legitimacy_candidate :=
  authority_basis
  + affected_scope_recognition
  + reason_giving
  + contestability
  + burden_accounting
  + correction_capacity
  + expiry_or_review
  + independent_challenge
```

This is not a final theory of political legitimacy. It is an anti-laundering structure.

Authority becomes more legitimate when it names its basis, recognizes affected scopes, gives reasons, preserves contestability, accounts for burden, remains correctable, expires or faces review where appropriate, and allows independent challenge.

Authority becomes more suspect when it hides its basis, erases affected scopes, refuses reasons, blocks challenge, routes burden downward, makes correction unreachable, extends itself without review, or judges only its own conduct.

For the value spine, legitimacy also asks whether authority preserves the conditions under which affected scopes can orient, contest, repair, and continue.

```trace
legitimacy_value_candidate :=
  authority_changes_viable_becoming
  + authority_names_basis
  + affected_scope_can_orient
  + affected_scope_can_contest_where_possible
  + burden_and_residue_recorded
  + correction_remains_live
```

The anti-circularity rule is strict:

```trace
legitimacy_cannot_mean :=
  authority_is_legitimate
  because_legitimate_authority_used_it
```

That is empty. Power cannot validate itself by naming itself valid.

Legitimacy must show its route.

## 11.3 Coercion Under Uncertainty

Coercion is action that constrains another scope's options, reachability, or conduct through force, threat, dependency, or structured lack of alternative.

```trace
coercion :=
  imposed_constraint
  on_scope_options_or_reachability
  under_pressure_or_threat_or_dependency
```

Some coercion is plainly abusive. Some is ordinary and lawful. Some may be necessary to prevent greater harm. A quarantine order, arrest, child protection intervention, evacuation, treatment refusal, platform suspension, school exclusion, emergency closure, or ecological control may all involve coercive elements.

Mechanical Ethics cannot simply say "no coercion."

But coercion under uncertainty carries special danger. The actor may be wrong about the threat. The affected scope may be unable to contest. The loss may harden before correction. The coercive system may benefit from treating its own estimate as final.

So coercion needs stronger audit:

```trace
coercion_audit :=
  threat_or_reason
  + affected_scope
  + evidence_status
  + uncertainty_status
  + less_coercive_options
  + timing_pressure
  + contestability
  + expiry_or_review
  + residue
```

Coercion may sometimes be justified. But coercion should not be cleanly justified by the fact that authority found it convenient, efficient, traditional, or lawful.

The more coercive the action, the more serious the duty to preserve correction and record residue.

The value-spine question is whether the constraint protects viable becoming, damages it, or both.

```trace
coercion_value_audit :=
  what_closure_is_imposed
  + what_viable_becoming_is_claimed_to_be_protected
  + what_viable_becoming_is_damaged
  + whether_less_closing_route_exists
  + what_repair_or_residue_follows
```

A constraint may be defensible because it prevents faster or wider closure. But if it does so, the record must still name the closure it imposes.

### 11.3.1 Hard-No Constraints for Coercion and Force

This part does not solve legitimacy. But it can still name some hard-no constraints.

```trace
coercion_hard_no_constraints :=
  no_coercion_from_erased_scope
  + no_irreversible_coercion_from_unchecked_contaminated_estimate
  + no_force_without_named_authority_basis
  + no_continuing_force_after_basis_expires
  + no_emergency_power_without_expiry_or_review
  + no_punishment_from_visibility_alone
  + no_coercion_that_requires_false_cleanliness
```

These constraints are negative boundaries, not a positive theory of justified force.

They say that coercive power fails before the harder legitimacy question is even reached if it depends on erased affected scopes, unchecked contaminated evidence, unnamed authority, expired basis, emergency without expiry, visibility-as-blame, or cleaned dirty loss.

If one of these hard-no constraints is triggered, the likely router finding is `HALT`, `HOLD`, or `ESCALATE`, not `PROCEED_ONLY_WITH_NAMED_CONSTRAINTS`, unless an explicit dirty-conflict emergency chain explains why immediate constrained action is still required.

The burden is on the actor using power to show the route. The burden is not on the affected scope to prove that invisible power is illegitimate.

## 11.4 Emergency Power

Emergency compresses time.

Some emergencies are real. Fire, flood, violence, epidemic, collapse, acute medical risk, safeguarding danger, ecological crisis, or infrastructure failure may require fast action before ordinary process can operate fully.

Mechanical Ethics does not deny emergency.

It denies emergency as amnesia.

```trace
emergency_power :=
  extraordinary_constraint
  justified_by_time_compressed_threat
  requiring_expiry_review_and_residue_record
```

Emergency power should answer at least four questions.

First: what is the immediate threat?

Second: what ordinary safeguards are being suspended, narrowed, or delayed?

Third: what expiry, review, or correction channel remains?

Fourth: did prior failure help produce the emergency?

The fourth question matters because institutions often create or worsen emergency through neglect. Maintenance is ignored. Warnings are dismissed. capacity is underbuilt. People are left without early routes. Then the crisis arrives, and extraordinary power is claimed as if it came from nowhere.

Mechanical Ethics says emergency may justify speed, but it does not erase history.

```trace
emergency_must_not_launder :=
  prior_warning
  + prior_neglect
  + underbuilt_correction
  + avoidable_hardening
```

Emergency action may be necessary and still dirty.

The record should be strong enough to say both.

Emergency may also protect viable becoming where delay would close it. That is real. A rescue, evacuation, intervention, or temporary restriction may preserve life, capacity, trust, or future repairability. But this claimed good must not erase the burden imposed by the emergency route.

```trace
emergency_value_record :=
  what_viable_becoming_was_preserved
  + what_scope_was_constrained
  + why_time_pressed
  + what_review_or_expiry_remains
  + what_residue_follows
```

## 11.5 Punishment and Desert

Punishment and desert are not solved here.

They are too dangerous to smuggle in through the side door.

Desert asks what someone deserves because of what they did, intended, risked, ignored, or became responsible for. Punishment asks whether and how a system may intentionally impose burden, pain, deprivation, stigma, restriction, or loss in response.

Mechanical Ethics has tools relevant to punishment: visibility, decision-time evidence, role duty, meaningful control, responsibility attachment, residue, repair, prevention, and legitimacy. But those tools do not by themselves settle desert.

```trace
punishment_desert_status :=
  UNRESOLVED_VALUE_CRUX
  unless_structural_chain_and_normative_authority_are_explicit
```

This part can still set constraints.

Punishment must not be based on visibility alone.

Punishment must not treat prediction as destiny.

Punishment must not use one visible person to hide system design.

Punishment must not pretend to repair if it only satisfies anger or institutional closure.

Punishment must not continue after its authority, purpose, or evidence has expired.

Punishment must preserve correction where error is possible.

```trace
punishment_constraints :=
  no_visibility_as_blame
  + no_prediction_as_destiny
  + no_scapegoating
  + no_repair_theatre
  + correction_route_required
  + expiry_or_review_required_where_power_continues
```

These are constraints, not a full theory.

The book must be honest: it can narrow what punishment may not hide. It has not yet proven what punishment positively deserves.

For the value spine, punishment also must not pretend that imposed burden is automatically repair. If punishment is claimed to protect future viable becoming, that claim needs a route: prevention, restoration, accountability, safety, rehabilitation, recognition, or recurrence reduction. If no such route is shown, punishment is only a burden with a story.

```trace
punishment_value_audit :=
  burden_imposed
  + claimed_future_protection_or_repair
  + evidence_for_that_route
  + correction_if_wrong
  + residue_record
```

## 11.6 Dignity and Recognition

Dignity is also dangerous to define too quickly.

It is easy to use dignity as a noble word with no mechanism. It is also easy to avoid dignity because it resists measurement.

Mechanical Ethics can approach dignity through recognition.

A scope is denied recognition when it is treated as if its future-space, account, suffering, agency, dependency, or residue need not appear in the record.

```trace
recognition_failure :=
  affected_scope_exists
  + affected_scope_materially_changed
  + record_or_process_erases_that_scope
```

This is not all that dignity means. But it is one mechanical way dignity is violated.

A person made to navigate an opaque system that does not hear them is denied recognition. A prisoner treated only as risk may be denied recognition. A patient treated only as throughput may be denied recognition. A worker treated only as metric may be denied recognition. An animal treated only as object may be denied recognition where sentience or agency is relevant. A community treated only as obstacle may be denied recognition.

Recognition does not mean the scope always gets what it wants.

It means the scope's presence, account, and future-space cannot be erased for administrative convenience.

```trace
dignity_minimum_candidate :=
  do_not_reduce_scope_to_system_variable
  + preserve_account_where_material
  + give_reasons_where_power_acts
  + preserve_contestability_where_possible
```

The value-spine version adds that a scope should not be reduced to a tool, obstacle, risk score, burden, case number, output, or optimization variable when its viable becoming is materially at stake.

```trace
dignity_as_viable_becoming_recognition :=
  scope_not_reduced_to_variable
  + account_preserved_where_material
  + viable_becoming_not_erased
  + reasons_given_where_power_shapes_path
  + route_back_preserved_where_possible
```

This remains a candidate. It should be tested, not canonized.

## 11.7 Authority That Expires

Power should not always continue just because it was once justified.

Authority can expire.

```trace
authority_expiry :=
  authority_basis_no_longer_matches
  threat_or_need_or_role_or_consent_or_evidence
```

Emergency authority may expire when the emergency passes. Coercive authority may expire when threat changes. Parental or custodial authority may change as capacity develops. Administrative authority may expire when a decision is corrected. Data-use authority may expire when consent, purpose, or necessity ends. Model deployment authority may expire when drift, failure, or contamination becomes visible.

Systems resist expiry. Power tends to preserve itself. Temporary measures become routine. Exceptional tools become normal. Safeguards suspended for crisis are not restored. Data gathered for one purpose becomes available for another. A risk label remains after risk changes.

Mechanical Ethics treats expiry as a legitimacy condition.

```trace
expiry_check :=
  original_basis
  + current_basis
  + changed_facts
  + affected_scope_burden
  + review_route
```

Authority should have memory of why it began and conditions for when it must narrow, stop, or return.

If power cannot state how it expires, it is already suspect.

Expiry protects viable becoming because ongoing power keeps shaping the field after its original basis has passed.

```trace
expiry_value_function :=
  prevent_power_from_becoming_default_world
  + return_future_space_where_basis_has_expired
  + reopen_contestability
  + reduce_unneeded_dependency
```

## 11.8 When Force May Be Used

This book should not pretend to give a complete theory of force.

Force may be physical, legal, economic, administrative, technical, or informational. It may restrain, compel, exclude, seize, confine, remove, disable, or override.

The question of justified force is a high-risk value-crux.

```trace
force_status :=
  UNRESOLVED_VALUE_CRUX
  unless_case_specific_chain_is_shown
```

Mechanical Ethics can frame conditions that any claimed force should answer:

```trace
force_audit :=
  threat_or_need
  + authority_basis
  + affected_scope_recognition
  + evidence_status
  + less_forceful_options
  + proportionality_or_floor_issue
  + contestability_where_possible
  + expiry_or_review
  + residue
```

Force becomes more suspect when the threat is vague, the evidence is contaminated, the affected scope cannot contest, less forceful routes were ignored, the actor benefits from force, or power continues after the reason expires.

Force may become more defensible where threat is immediate, evidence is strong, delay would close protection, less forceful routes are unavailable, and review or residue accounting remains.

But this is not a permission machine.

A force audit does not make force clean.

It shows what must be answered before force can claim legitimacy.

The value-spine audit asks whether force preserves viable becoming from a greater closure, and whose viable becoming it closes in doing so.

```trace
force_value_audit :=
  claimed_protected_future
  + imposed_closure
  + least_closing_available_route
  + review_or_expiry
  + residue
```

## 11.9 When Force Must Stop

Stopping force matters as much as authorizing it.

A system that can begin force but cannot stop it is structurally dangerous.

```trace
force_must_stop_when :=
  threat_basis_expires
  | evidence_collapses
  | less_forceful_route_becomes_available
  | correction_finds_error
  | residue_or_burden_becomes_disproportionate
  | authority_expiry_condition_met
```

This applies beyond physical force.

A suspension should stop when the risk basis collapses. A data restriction should stop when purpose expires. A coercive placement should stop when conditions no longer justify it. An emergency power should stop when emergency conditions pass. An enforcement route should pause when correction becomes materially credible. A model-based exclusion should stop when the estimate is shown contaminated or unfit.

Stopping is hard because force creates institutional momentum. Once a person is labelled, confined, excluded, flagged, suspected, or routed, the burden often shifts onto them to prove the force should end.

Mechanical Ethics resists that burden shift.

```trace
continuing_force_requires :=
  renewed_basis
  + review
  + affected_scope_route
  + residue_check
```

The fact that force was once justified does not prove it remains justified.

If force continues after it no longer protects viable becoming, it becomes closure for its own sake.

## 11.10 What This Part Has Not Solved

Part 11 must end with restraint.

It has framed power as capacity to change future-space and viable becoming. It has offered a candidate structure for legitimacy as answerable authority. It has described coercion under uncertainty, hard-no constraints for coercion and force, emergency power, punishment constraints, dignity through recognition, authority expiry, and force audits.

It has not solved political legitimacy.

It has not solved punishment.

It has not solved desert.

It has not solved dignity.

It has not solved all justified coercion.

It has not solved acceptable sacrifice.

```trace
Part_11_status :=
  framing_not_solving
  + high_UNRESOLVED_VALUE_CRUX_risk
  + anti_laundering_constraints_only
```

This is not failure. It is honesty.

The book has enough structure to say that power must answer for the future-space and viable becoming it changes. It does not yet have enough to claim final authority over every use of power.

The next part shifts scale.

If individual action and institutional power can narrow or preserve viable future, then civilization itself can be understood as an attempt to remain correctable under uncertainty.
