# Part 1 — The Entity That Wakes

Status: first draft. Incomplete. Not canon.

## 1.1 Something Is Here

Something is here.

That is the smallest usable beginning.

Not yet a person. Not yet a citizen. Not yet a moral patient. Not yet a rational agent. Not yet a soul. Not yet a machine. Not yet innocent or guilty. Not yet owed everything. Not yet owed nothing.

Only this:

```trace
origin_1 :=
  something_is_here
```

The word "something" is deliberately thin. It avoids pretending that we already know what kind of entity we are dealing with. It may be a human reader. It may be an animal. It may be an institution, a future system, or an artificial process capable of acting in ways that matter. It may be something whose standing is disputed.

Mechanical Ethics does not need to settle all of that at the first line.

It begins with presence. A thing is in the world. It can be affected. It may affect. It may continue. That is enough to start the inquiry, but not enough to finish it.

This matters because many ethical systems begin too late. They begin after personhood, law, preference, language, rationality, utility, rights, or social membership has already been assumed. Those assumptions may be useful later. They are not the beginning.

The beginning is thinner.

Something is here.

## 1.1.1 Good and Bad Before Morality

Before the book can speak about moral good or moral bad, it can speak about change.

A thing can continue or fail to continue. It can become more oriented or less oriented. It can gain capacity or lose capacity. It can become more able to repair after error or less able. It can enter relation or be isolated. It can have meaningful future-space opened or closed.

This is not yet a final moral theory. It is not yet a theory of rights, virtue, welfare, law, personhood, justice, or desert. It is only the first directional pressure in the field.

```trace
pre_moral_direction :=
  state_can_change
  + change_can_support_continuation_orientation_capacity_relation_repairability
  + change_can_damage_or_close_those_conditions
  + uncertainty_never_zero
```

Mechanical Ethics therefore uses careful language. It does not claim final access to the Good from the first page. It can say that a transition appears to support or damage the conditions under which an entity can continue, orient, learn, relate, repair, choose meaningfully, and become.

```trace
good_candidate :=
  transition
  that_appears_to_support_viable_becoming
  for_named_scope_or_scopes
  under_stated_uncertainty
  with_costs_and_residue_visible

bad_candidate :=
  transition
  that_appears_to_damage_or_close_viable_becoming
  for_named_scope_or_scopes
  under_stated_uncertainty
  especially_when_hidden_unrepairable_or_forced
```

The word `candidate` is not weakness. It is honesty. Every claim about good or bad must later carry scope, time, uncertainty, confidence, cost, and repair path.

The book will need this distinction throughout. A pleasant transition can narrow the future. A painful transition can restore orientation. A constraint can dominate, or it can preserve wider capacity. An expansion can liberate one scope while burdening another. So Mechanical Ethics cannot simply count options, rewards, or immediate feelings and call the result good.

For now, it is enough to mark the primitive:

```trace
viable_becoming :=
  continuation
  + orientation
  + capacity
  + relation
  + repairability
  + meaningful_future_space
  under_uncertainty
```

That is not the whole of ethics. It is the first value-bearing structure the book can honestly name.

## 1.2 It Perceives Partially

The thing that is here does not see everything.

It has an angle. It has a boundary. It receives some signals and misses others. It may know its own pressure better than another's. It may know another's pressure badly. It may know the immediate better than the distant. It may know what it wants better than what its wanting will do.

```trace
origin_2 :=
  perceives_partially
  + uncertainty_never_zero
```

Partial perception is not a defect added later. It is part of the starting condition.

This remains true even when the entity is intelligent. It remains true when it is expert. It remains true when it is powerful. It remains true when it has data, models, sensors, law, authority, witnesses, or confidence.

A powerful actor can still see partially. A bureaucracy can still see partially. A machine can still see partially. A person in pain can still see partially. A victim can see something real and still not see the whole system. An institution can see the system and still not see the wound.

This is why Mechanical Ethics treats certainty with suspicion. Not because every claim is equally weak, but because no actor should be allowed to convert its own partial view into the whole world without challenge.

The actor's own estimate of timing, burden, or repair may be incomplete. It may also be interested. If the actor caused, controls, or benefits from a loss, it is not a neutral judge of when that loss hardens.

That does not make the actor evil. It makes the actor non-final.

## 1.3 It Can Change Something

The thing that is here can change something.

It may move its body. It may choose a word. It may send a form. It may design a rule. It may maintain a system. It may delay a decision. It may refuse repair. It may trigger a model. It may set a clock. It may open a route or close one.

```trace
origin_3 :=
  can_change_something
```

This is where ethics begins to press, but it has not yet arrived in full.

A falling stone changes something. A storm changes something. A disease changes something. A machine process changes something. Not every change creates responsibility in the same way.

So Mechanical Ethics must not confuse causation with responsibility. Causation is wider. Responsibility is narrower and more structured. It requires more than effect. Later, the book will ask about visibility, role, control, decision-time evidence, and capacity to act.

For now, only the pressure is needed: action matters because it changes the field in which other continuations may or may not remain possible.

The smallest ethical danger is not malice. It is change under partial perception.

## 1.4 It Does Not Know Everything

The entity can act before it knows enough.

This is unavoidable. Waiting for perfect knowledge is also an action. Refusing to choose still changes the world. Delay can preserve possibility, but it can also close it.

```trace
origin_4 :=
  action_under_uncertainty
```

This means Mechanical Ethics cannot be a simple command to wait. It also cannot be a simple command to proceed.

The first discipline is not paralysis. It is honesty about what is known, what is unknown, who is estimating, who benefits from the estimate, who can challenge it, and what may harden before correction arrives.

When uncertainty is low and reversibility is high, action may be ordinary. When uncertainty is high and irreversibility is near, action needs stronger justification. When delay itself causes irreversible loss, non-action needs justification too.

This is the first hard shape:

```trace
uncertainty_pressure :=
  unknowns
  + action_effect
  + time_passing
  + reversibility_or_irreversibility
```

Uncertainty does not erase duty. It changes duty.

## 1.5 Time Passes

Time is not background.

Time changes what action means.

A correction that arrives before loss hardens is different from a correction that arrives after the person has lost housing, health, work, trust, evidence, or opportunity. A warning before a door closes is different from a warning after the door has closed. A review route available in theory is different from a review route that cannot be reached in time.

```trace
origin_5 :=
  time_passes
```

Mechanical Ethics treats timing as part of the mechanism, not decoration around the mechanism.

The same decision can be tolerable at one speed and intolerable at another. The same power can be bounded if challenge arrives in time and abusive if challenge arrives too late. The same correction can be meaningful before hardening and insulting after residue has become permanent.

This is one of the book's central claims: delay is often where harm hides.

If a system can act quickly but correct slowly, then power outruns answerability. If a rule can impose immediately but review later, the exposed person carries the system's uncertainty. If an automated decision can close access before a human can contest it, the route is not merely inefficient. It is ethically structured against correction.

Time does not only measure harm. Time participates in harm.

## 1.6 Inaction Also Changes the World

Doing nothing is not outside the system.

If a harmful path is already moving, refusal to intervene may let it harden. If an opportunity is closing, delay may destroy it. If an error is visible, non-correction may become part of the mechanism. If a person is carrying a burden, waiting may route the cost onto them.

```trace
inaction :=
  transition
  if future_space_changes
```

This matters because many actors hide inside non-action. They say they did not decide. They waited for evidence. They followed the process. They allowed the existing rule to continue. Sometimes that is responsible. Sometimes it is not.

Mechanical Ethics does not say action is always better than inaction. It says both must be visible.

The question is not, "Did someone push a button?"

The question is, "What changed because this actor acted, delayed, refused, maintained, ignored, delegated, or allowed the path to continue?"

That is the end of the first movement.

Something is here. It perceives partially. It can change something. It does not know everything. Time passes. Inaction also changes the world.

From that point, the next problem appears: the entity is not alone.
