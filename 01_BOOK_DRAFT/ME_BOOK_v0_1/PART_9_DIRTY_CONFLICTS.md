# Part 9 — Dirty Conflicts

Status: first draft. Incomplete. Not canon.

## 9.1 When Every Action Causes Loss

Some cases do not have a clean path.

Action causes loss. Inaction causes loss. Delay causes loss. Speed causes loss. Protection of one scope narrows another. Refusal to choose lets a harmful path continue. Choosing distributes harm through human hands.

Mechanical Ethics calls this a dirty conflict.

```trace
dirty_conflict :=
  every_available_path
  causes_material_loss
```

Dirty conflict is not ordinary inconvenience. It is not every disagreement. It is not a case where someone dislikes an outcome. It is a case where all reachable options impose material future-space contraction on at least one affected scope.

This is where ethical language often becomes dishonest.

People want the action to be clean because the action may be necessary. Institutions want the action to be clean because they need authority to act. Movements want the action to be clean because hesitation feels like betrayal. Experts want the action to be clean because the model selects a path. States want the action to be clean because emergency needs speed.

But necessity does not clean loss.

```trace
necessary_harm
  != clean_harm
```

A dirty conflict may still require action. Mechanical Ethics does not say that dirty means forbidden. It says dirty means the loss must not disappear.

## 9.2 Why Clean Answers Are Usually Lies

Clean answers are attractive.

They reduce guilt. They simplify communication. They protect authority. They let institutions proceed. They give the public a story. They give the actor a shield.

In dirty conflict, clean answers usually hide something.

They hide the sacrificed scope. They hide uncertainty. They hide the value tilt. They hide the nonparticipant. They hide the least repairable loss. They hide the fact that the chosen path may be best only under a specific priority that should be named.

```trace
cleaning_failure :=
  dirty_loss
  relabelled_as
  solved_or_costless_or_deserved_or_invisible
```

Mechanical Ethics refuses clean language where loss remains.

If a population is culled to protect an ecosystem, say what is killed and why. If a patient is deprioritized to save more patients, say what residue remains. If a city project displaces a community to improve infrastructure, say who carries the contraction. If a model rejects some applicants to reduce fraud, say who bears false positives and how they can contest. If emergency powers restrict liberty to prevent wider harm, say what liberty is restricted and how power expires.

The point is not to paralyse action.

The point is to keep action answerable.

## 9.3 No Netting Without Permission

One scope's gain does not automatically cancel another scope's loss.

```trace
no_netting_without_permission :=
  expansion_for_A
  does_not_automatically_cancel
  contraction_for_B
```

Netting is sometimes unavoidable. Public decisions often compare harms and benefits across groups. Triage compares outcomes. Infrastructure compares costs and gains. Ecology compares interdependent losses. Law and policy regularly act across plural interests.

Mechanical Ethics does not ban comparison.

It bans hidden comparison.

If one scope is narrowed for another's expansion, the record should say so. If the system treats the trade as justified, the value chain should be visible. If consent is absent, that absence should be visible. If compensation is offered, it should not be described as erasure. If the harmed scope cannot contest, that should raise caution.

```trace
netting_audit :=
  whose_gain
  + whose_loss
  + who_decided
  + by_what_authority
  + what_consent_or_contest
  + what_repair_or_residue
```

The danger is not only bad arithmetic. The danger is moral disappearance.

A net benefit can hide a destroyed life. A public gain can hide an unchosen burden. A statistical improvement can hide a class of false positives. An ecological necessity can hide the suffering of intelligent animals. A future benefit can hide present coercion.

Netting may sometimes be justified.

It must not be silent.

## 9.4 The Least-Repairable Loss

In dirty conflict, not all losses have the same repair profile.

Some can be compensated. Some can be reversed. Some can be reduced later. Some create permanent absence. Some remove time that cannot be returned. Some destroy trust. Some change identity, body, home, family, memory, or species continuity.

Mechanical Ethics therefore asks for the least-repairable loss.

```trace
least_repairable_loss :=
  loss_with_lowest_realistic_restoration_path
```

This is not neutral. It carries a value tilt toward reversibility and repairability.

The tilt is deliberate, but it must be declared.

```trace
choose_least_unrepairable_path_if_action_required :=
  declared_value_tilt
  + requires_its_own_audit_chain
```

Why privilege repairability at all?

Because action under uncertainty should preserve the possibility of correction where it can. When we do not know enough, destroying the route back is especially serious. A reversible burden may be preferable to an irreversible destruction, even if neither is clean.

But the rule is not absolute.

Some irreversible losses may be accepted to prevent wider or deeper irreversible losses. Some reversible burdens may be unjust because they are imposed repeatedly on the same vulnerable scope. Some repairable harms may still be degrading, coercive, or illegitimate. Some losses are incommensurable.

So least-repairable loss is a question, not an automatic answer.

## 9.5 The Least-Reachable Scope

Dirty conflicts often harm those least able to reach the decision.

The people not in the room. The animals outside the forum. The future persons not yet born. The users without appeal. The patient without specialist language. The claimant without legal help. The community without political power. The worker without bargaining power. The ecosystem without a representative.

```trace
least_reachable_scope :=
  affected_scope
  with_lowest_practical_access_to
  information_or_contest_or_repair
```

This matters because formal equality can hide practical exclusion.

A process may be open to all and reachable by few. A consultation may invite everyone and hear only the organized. A complaint channel may exist but require literacy, time, money, safety, or expertise. A public policy may count all affected groups but take evidence only from those who can produce it in the expected form.

Mechanical Ethics asks: who cannot reach the route that claims to protect them?

The least-reachable scope should not automatically win every conflict. That would be too simple. But if a dirty decision burdens a scope that cannot contest, the decision carries a higher duty of representation, caution, disclosure, and residue.

```trace
least_reachable_scope_pressure :=
  low_access
  + material_loss
  -> heightened_duty_to_record_represent_and_repair
```

Dirty conflict becomes more dangerous when the harmed scope cannot even appear as harmed.

## 9.6 Consent, Compensation, and Burden Return

Consent can change dirty conflict, but it rarely cleans it completely.

A person may consent to risk. A community may accept disruption for shared benefit. A patient may accept a treatment tradeoff. A worker may accept a temporary burden. A citizen may accept some restriction under emergency.

But consent must be real enough to carry weight.

```trace
consent_weight_depends_on :=
  information
  + real_alternative
  + low_coercion
  + capacity
  + ability_to_withdraw_or_contest
```

Where consent is absent, weaker, coerced, or impossible, the dirty residue increases.

Compensation also matters but does not erase everything.

Money may repair some reachability. It may acknowledge burden. It may reduce future contraction. It may be owed. But money does not always return time, health, trust, home, lost childhood, ecological destruction, bodily integrity, or life.

```trace
compensation != erasure
```

Burden return is often more important than symbolic closure.

If the system created the conflict, the system should not offload all navigation, proof, waiting, cost, or repair onto the affected scope. If a dirty action is necessary, the actor may owe ongoing burden return: help, access, explanation, monitoring, compensation, mitigation, and recurrence prevention.

The question is not only, "Was the sacrifice justified?"

It is also, "Who carries the aftermath?"

## 9.7 Triage Without Moral Disappearance

Triage is one of the hardest dirty conflicts.

Medical triage, emergency response, disaster allocation, ecological intervention, infrastructure planning, platform moderation, wartime evacuation, child protection, scarce housing, scarce benefits, scarce attention — all can force choices where not everyone can receive what they need.

Mechanical Ethics cannot make triage clean.

It can demand that triage not erase.

```trace
triage_discipline :=
  name_affected_scopes
  + name_scarcity_or_conflict
  + name_selection_criteria
  + preserve_challenge_where_possible
  + record_residue
  + prevent_hidden_worth_ladder
```

A triage rule must not secretly decide that some people count less while publicly claiming neutral procedure. It must not use technical criteria to hide social worth judgments. It must not make the excluded disappear because the selected path helped more people.

This is difficult because triage often requires speed.

Emergency triage cannot always provide full deliberation. But speed does not remove the need for later record. If decisions must be made quickly, the residue and criteria should be recorded after. If criteria were wrong, repair should follow where possible. If some losses could not be repaired, they should remain visible.

Triage is where Mechanical Ethics must be most disciplined and least theatrical.

## 9.8 Residue After Forced Loss

Forced loss leaves residue even when justified.

A person lawfully detained has lost liberty. A patient denied a scarce treatment has lost a chance. A community displaced for flood protection has lost place. An animal killed for ecological protection has lost life. A future person inheriting a narrowed world has lost options they never had a chance to defend.

Justification may change guilt. It may change duty. It may make action necessary. It may prevent greater harm.

It does not erase residue.

```trace
justified_loss
  may_reduce_wrongdoing
  but_does_not_erase_residue
```

Residue after forced loss should include the loss itself, who imposed it, why it was imposed, what alternatives were rejected, what repair or compensation is possible, what remains impossible to restore, and what future constraint follows.

A dirty decision without residue record trains the actor to become cleaner in its own eyes each time it acts.

That is dangerous.

Moral injury can also be residue. The actor forced to choose may carry damage. The witness may carry damage. The institution may carry trust damage. These do not replace the affected scope's loss, but they matter for future answerability.

A system that repeatedly forces dirty choices should not merely celebrate decisive action. It should ask why dirty choice became routine.

## 9.9 When ME Must Hold or Escalate

Mechanical Ethics must be able to refuse to decide.

Some dirty conflicts exceed the current record. Some depend on unresolved value-cruxes. Some require democratic authority, legal process, specialist evidence, affected-scope representation, or independent review. Some are too contaminated by the actor's interest. Some carry irreversible loss under unknown facts.

```trace
ME_must_not_fake_decision_when :=
  material_unknowns
  + irreversible_action
  + unresolved_value_crux
  + contaminated_authority
  + inadequate_scope_representation
```

The proper output may be `HOLD`.

The proper output may be `ESCALATE`.

The proper output may be `UNRESOLVED_VALUE_CRUX`.

The proper output may be `PROCEED_ONLY_WITH_NAMED_CONSTRAINTS` if delay itself causes greater loss and the constraints are real.

A framework that cannot hold is not ethics. It is acceleration.

A framework that always holds is not ethics either. It is paralysis.

Dirty conflict is the place where Mechanical Ethics must show discipline: neither cleaning loss nor refusing responsibility merely because the answer is not pure.

Part 9 ends with the hardest practical instruction so far:

```trace
dirty_conflict_instruction :=
  act_only_if_needed
  + name_loss
  + name_value_tilt
  + protect_least_reachable
  + preserve_minimum_floors_where_known
  + record_residue
  + assign_repair_duties
  + hold_or_escalate_when_not_safe_to_route
```

The next part turns from what should happen to who must answer.

Visibility is not blame. But visibility can create responsibility.
