# Part 7 — Protection, Standing, and Scope

Status: first draft. Incomplete. Not canon.

## 7.1 Scope Is Not Personhood

The first mistake is to think that protection begins only after personhood is settled.

Personhood matters. It may become central in many cases. But if Mechanical Ethics waits for final personhood before naming affected scopes, then disputed beings, future beings, non-human beings, dependent beings, and structurally silent beings can be harmed while the argument is still waiting for permission to notice them.

So this book separates scope from personhood.

```trace
scope != personhood
```

A scope is something whose future-space can be affected in a way that matters for the case record.

A person is one kind of scope. Not the only possible kind.

A family can be a scope. A worker group can be a scope. A disabled person facing an inaccessible process can be a scope. A future child can be a scope. An animal population can be a scope. A river system can be a scope. An institution may be a scope when it carries memory, trust, or correction capacity for people. An artificial system may become a disputed scope if its behaviour raises standing questions that cannot be dismissed cheaply.

This does not mean every scope receives equal protection.

It means a scope should not disappear before the protection question is asked.

```trace
affected_scope :=
  entity_or_group_or_system
  whose_future_space_may_be_materially_changed
```

The language is intentionally broad at the intake stage. Broad intake is not broad permission. It is anti-erasure.

## 7.2 Reasons for Protection

Protection needs reasons.

Mechanical Ethics should not create a hidden worth ladder where the writer silently knows who counts and who does not. It should record why a scope receives protection or precaution.

Possible reasons include sentience, vulnerability, dependency, participation, consent status, inability to contest, exposure to irreversible loss, role in sustaining other protected scopes, future-space contraction, or uncertainty about standing under conditions where error would be costly.

```trace
protection_reason :=
  sentience_signal
  | vulnerability
  | dependency
  | participation_interest
  | consent_or_nonconsent
  | inability_to_contest
  | irreversible_loss_exposure
  | future_space_contraction
  | support_role_for_other_scopes
  | standing_uncertainty_with_high_error_cost
```

This list is not final doctrine. It is a record discipline.

A human child receives strong protection for many reasons at once: sentience, vulnerability, dependency, future-space, inability to fully contest, and social recognition. An adult wrongfully routed by a system receives protection because their options, reachability, information, and dignity may be narrowed under a structure they cannot easily challenge. A non-human animal may receive protection because it has sentience, agency, sociality, learning, fear, preference, or vulnerability. A future person may receive protection because present action can close paths before they exist to object.

A scope can also receive weak or precautionary protection.

Protection does not always mean stop. It may mean disclose, monitor, slow, preserve evidence, reduce burden, maintain contestability, avoid irreversible action, or record residue.

The point is not to inflate every scope into full moral equality. The point is to make the protection route visible.

### 7.2.1 Minimum Floor Candidate

Mechanical Ethics still has an open wound about final standing. It has not proven a complete foundation for every protected scope.

But uncertainty about final standing must not mean that anything unresolved is expendable. The first floor can be modest:

```trace
minimum_floor_candidate :=
  no_erasure
  + no_unrecorded_irreversible_loss
  + no_burden_without_route
  + no_silent_standing_denial
  + no_clean_language_for_dirty_loss
```

This floor does not grant equal status to every scope. It does not settle personhood, animal standing, ecosystem standing, future-person claims, or artificial moral status.

It says something narrower: if a scope may be materially affected, the system must not erase it, impose irreversible loss without record, shift burden without a route, deny standing silently, or clean the loss through language.

The floor is strongest when four pressures combine:

```trace
floor_strengthens_when :=
  vulnerability
  + low_contest_power
  + irreversibility
  + actor_control_over_route
```

This is a candidate floor, not canon. It is here to stop the open wound becoming a loophole.

## 7.3 Uncertainty Raises Caution

Standing uncertainty is not permission to spend cheaply.

If we do not know whether a scope matters, the answer is not automatically "therefore it does not matter." That is an error rule, and a dangerous one. It gives power to the side that benefits from uncertainty.

```trace
standing_uncertainty :=
  unknown_status
  + possible_material_loss
  + asymmetric_error_cost
```

When the cost of wrongly denying protection is high, uncertainty should raise caution.

This does not mean every uncertain scope receives full protection. That would create paralysis and could erase other scopes. It means uncertainty must be recorded and routed.

Mechanical Ethics can use graduated responses:

```trace
uncertainty_response :=
  record_dispute
  + avoid_irreversible_loss_where_possible
  + seek_better_evidence
  + preserve_future_contestability
  + use_precaution_when_error_cost_high
```

Consider a disputed artificial system. It may not be a moral patient. Most current systems may not deserve standing in any strong sense. But if a future system shows persistent self-protective behaviour, suffering-like reports, social dependence, or complex preference continuity, it would be reckless to treat uncertainty as proof of non-standing. The answer may be investigation, constraint, and precaution, not immediate equality.

Consider animals. A system that treats animal intelligence as irrelevant because animals cannot enter the human legal forum is not being neutral. It is using forum membership as a worth filter.

Consider future persons. They cannot object now. That silence cannot be used as consent.

Uncertainty does not settle protection. It changes the burden of care.

## 7.4 Vulnerability and Dependency

Vulnerability changes the ethical weight of a transition.

A vulnerable scope is not merely one that can be harmed. All scopes can be harmed in some way. Vulnerability means a scope has reduced ability to absorb, avoid, understand, contest, or repair a narrowing of future-space.

```trace
vulnerability :=
  low_buffer
  | low_contest_power
  | high_dependency
  | high_exposure_to_irreversibility
  | low_repair_capacity
```

Dependency matters because it makes the actor's conduct more dangerous.

A child depends on adults. A patient depends on a medical system. A benefit claimant depends on administrative timing. A prisoner depends on state custody. A worker may depend on a platform or employer record. An animal in a human-shaped environment may depend on human restraint. A community downstream depends on decisions made upstream.

Dependency does not make the dependent scope morally pure. It does not remove all duty from the dependent actor. But it changes what the powerful actor may claim.

The more one scope depends on another's process, the less acceptable it is for that process to be opaque, slow, unreachable, or self-certifying.

```trace
dependency_pressure :=
  actor_controls_route
  + affected_scope_depends_on_route
  -> higher_duty_of_disclosure_contestability_and_timely_repair
```

Vulnerability and dependency are not sentimental categories. They are mechanical facts about who can survive delay, burden, uncertainty, and error.

## 7.5 Participation and Consent

Consent matters, but consent is not magic.

A scope that participates in a transition may carry different duties and risks than a scope that is dragged into it. But participation must be real enough to count.

```trace
consent_question :=
  did_scope_have_information
  + real_alternative
  + capacity
  + low_coercion
  + ability_to_withdraw_or_contest
```

Mechanical Ethics should not treat formal acceptance as full consent if the affected scope lacked information, realistic alternatives, or freedom from pressure.

A worker may "agree" to a condition because refusal means unemployment. A patient may "agree" because the system withheld practical options. A user may "agree" to terms no one can read. A community may "consult" after the decision has effectively been made. An animal cannot consent in human form. A future person cannot consent at all.

Consent can reduce certain burdens. It can authorize risk. It can make shared projects possible. But it cannot be used to hide asymmetry.

Consent also has timing. Consent before information changes is not consent to everything later discovered. Consent under one risk profile is not consent under another. Consent to participate is not consent to unrepaired residue.

```trace
consent_is_bounded_by :=
  information
  + scope
  + time
  + coercion_level
  + withdrawal_or_contestability
```

This becomes important in dirty conflicts and institutional settings, where people are often counted as participants in systems they cannot practically leave.

## 7.6 Non-Human Life

Non-human life is not a decoration around human ethics.

Animals act, learn, fear, prefer, bond, explore, avoid, remember, and suffer in different ways. Some show complex social behaviour and flexible intelligence. Ecosystems carry interdependent continuations that no single organism owns. Human systems routinely change these futures at scale.

Mechanical Ethics should not pretend this is simple.

```trace
nonhuman_scope_questions :=
  sentience_signal
  + agency_signal
  + dependency_on_human_systems
  + ecological_role
  + vulnerability
  + irreversibility_of_loss
```

This does not mean non-human life always overrides human safety, food, health, shelter, or ecological protection. It does mean non-human loss should not be laundered as mere management when the affected beings or systems have relevant continuation interests.

A dirty conflict involving intelligent invasive animals is still dirty. Ecological protection may require killing. Human safety may require control. Crop protection may be legitimate. But the animals do not become morally invisible because the management need is real.

The record should say what is being protected, what is being killed, what alternatives were considered, what suffering is imposed, what residue remains, and whether human-caused conditions helped create the conflict.

The aim is not purity. The aim is not sentimental denial of ecological reality.

The aim is to stop necessary harm from becoming clean language.

## 7.7 Future Persons

Future persons cannot object.

They cannot file a complaint, attend a meeting, vote in the present, correct a record, or tell us which losses they would have accepted. This makes them easy to erase.

```trace
future_scope :=
  not_present_to_contest
  + affected_by_present_transition
  + possible_future_space_contraction
```

Mechanical Ethics should not treat absence as consent.

The difficulty is that future persons are not a single known group with clear present preferences. Claims about them can be abused. A state can invoke future generations to justify present coercion. An institution can claim long-term benefit while imposing unrecorded loss now. A movement can use imagined future approval to silence actual affected people.

So future-person reasoning needs discipline.

It should be strongest where present action creates irreversible or long-lasting future-space contraction, especially where those future scopes will have no realistic repair route.

It should be weaker where the claim is speculative, reversible, or used to erase present affected scopes without an audit chain.

```trace
future_person_caution :=
  stronger_when_irreversible
  + stronger_when_repair_unavailable
  + weaker_when_speculative_or_laundering_present_power
```

Future persons matter. They are also easy to weaponize. Both truths must remain visible.

## 7.8 Artificial Systems

Artificial systems create two separate questions.

The first is easy: artificial systems can affect protected scopes and therefore must be constrained.

The second is hard: some artificial systems might eventually become protected scopes themselves.

Mechanical Ethics should not collapse these questions.

```trace
AI_question_split :=
  constrain_AI_as_actor_or_tool
  != grant_AI_moral_standing
```

A present model used to deny benefits, allocate medical risk, route police attention, score workers, recommend content, or operate infrastructure can be ethically significant even if it has no moral standing at all. The duties may attach to the people and institutions that design, deploy, maintain, and benefit from it.

That is the first question: acting systems under human delegation must not hide loss, uncertainty, burden, or correction failure behind technical opacity.

The second question concerns possible future minds or artificial subjects. This remains unresolved. Mechanical Ethics should not grant standing casually. It should also not build a framework that makes future standing impossible to recognize because the category was excluded in advance.

A cautious position is this:

```trace
artificial_standing_status :=
  currently_unresolved
  + precaution_when_signals_and_error_cost_require
  + no_default_full_standing
  + no_default_erasure
```

The book can constrain artificial systems before deciding whether any artificial system counts as a protected scope.

That distinction must remain clean.

## 7.9 When Standing Remains Unresolved

Some standing questions will remain unresolved.

That is not a defect if the unresolved status is honest.

Mechanical Ethics should have outputs for unresolved standing:

```trace
standing_outputs :=
  protection_triggered
  | precaution_triggered
  | standing_unresolved
  | no_protection_reason_found
```

`No protection reason found` is not the same as "worthless." It means the current record has not shown a reason for special protection under Mechanical Ethics.

`Standing unresolved` is not the same as "ignore." It means the case may require hold, escalation, narrower action, evidence gathering, or precaution depending on the risk.

`Precaution triggered` is not the same as full equality. It means error costs or uncertainty justify constraint.

`Protection triggered` is not the end of the case. It means duties now have to be derived.

The open wound remains:

```trace
open_wound :=
  final_floor_foundation_for_scope_protection
```

Mechanical Ethics needs minimum floors, but it has not yet fully solved who sets them, how they are justified, and how they operate across humans, animals, ecosystems, future persons, and artificial systems.

The minimum floor candidate above reduces the immediate loophole but does not close the wound. It says unresolved standing cannot justify erasure or unrecorded irreversible loss. It does not prove a final hierarchy of protection.

The honest move is not to hide that wound. The honest move is to carry it forward and prevent it from being silently settled by whoever has power.

Part 7 therefore ends with a limited achievement.

It does not solve moral standing.

It gives the book a way to name affected scopes, record protection reasons, raise caution under uncertainty, preserve a minimum anti-erasure floor, and avoid the worst erasures before duties are assigned.

The next question is what happens after harm, error, or dirty loss has already entered the path.

That is correction, repair, and residue.
