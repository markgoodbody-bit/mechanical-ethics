# Part 3 — Layers of Action

Status: first draft. Incomplete. Not canon.

## 3.1 The Causal Layer

Things cause things.

A stone falls. A river floods. A fire burns. A wall blocks. A form rejects. A model classifies. A rule routes. A silence delays. A maintenance failure lets a path continue.

Causation is the widest layer.

```trace
causal_layer :=
  event_or_state
  changes
  another_event_or_state
```

Mechanical Ethics must begin here but must not stop here.

If everything that causes is responsible, then responsibility becomes meaningless. Storms become guilty. Stones become negligent. Disease becomes blameworthy. Every condition becomes an accused actor.

That is wrong.

But the opposite mistake is also wrong: treating causal machinery as ethically irrelevant because no one intended the harm at the final moment. Institutions often hide there. Automated systems hide there. Bureaucracies hide there. They turn responsibility into a search for a single malicious hand, and when no such hand appears, the machine is allowed to continue.

Mechanical Ethics needs a stricter distinction.

Causation identifies the path. Responsibility comes later, when visibility, role, control, timing, and capacity enter the record.

So the first causal question is not, "Who is guilty?"

It is:

```trace
causal_question :=
  what_changed
  + through_what_path
  + affecting_whom
  + before_what_hardening
```

This keeps the path visible without turning every cause into blame.

## 3.2 The Selective Layer

Some things do more than cause.

They select.

They avoid, pursue, repeat, adapt, filter, prefer, reward, punish, optimize, or learn. They change their future action based on pressure or signal. They may not understand what they are doing. They may not reflect. But they are no longer merely falling stones.

```trace
selective_layer :=
  signal
  + option_set
  + selection
  + consequence
  + update_or_repetition
```

Selection matters because it creates direction.

An organism seeks food. A company seeks profit. A platform seeks engagement. A regulator seeks compliance. A model seeks lower loss. A habit seeks relief. A bureaucracy seeks case closure. A political system seeks votes. A person seeks love, safety, dominance, comfort, meaning, or escape.

None of this is automatically bad.

Local reward is how many systems stay alive and coherent. Hunger pulls the animal toward food. Pain pulls the body away from injury. Pleasure teaches repetition. Praise trains behaviour. Metrics focus institutions. Loss functions tune models.

The danger is not that systems are pulled.

The danger is that local reward can diverge from long-term viability, repairability, truth, or another scope's future-space.

```trace
local_reward_pull != long_term_viability
```

A system can become very good at getting what it is rewarded for and worse at preserving what matters. A complaint process can learn to close complaints. A platform can learn to capture attention. A workplace can learn to protect its metrics. A model can learn a proxy. A person can learn a coping behaviour that narrows the future.

Selection gives the book a way to speak about harmful systems without pretending they are demons.

They may be doing exactly what they were trained, rewarded, selected, or permitted to do.

That is not absolution. It is diagnosis.

## 3.3 The Reflective Layer

Some entities can model the path.

They can ask what they are doing. They can imagine another outcome. They can receive a warning. They can recognize that a rule is failing. They can understand that a person is being harmed. They can change course because of a reason.

```trace
reflective_layer :=
  self_model
  + other_model
  + time_model
  + reason_response
  + possible_course_change
```

Reflection does not require perfect wisdom. It does not require pure morality. It does not require full self-transparency.

It requires enough capacity to treat the action as answerable rather than merely happening.

This is where responsibility begins to press harder.

A storm does not answer. A stone does not answer. A child may answer in a limited way. An adult may answer more fully. An institution answers through records, roles, escalation routes, duties, and correction capacities. An artificial system may not answer morally in its own right, but the people and institutions deploying it may have made a reflective system of delegation.

The reflective layer is not a magic border. It is graded. It can be damaged, partial, distributed, or staged.

But once a system has warning, record, role, and capacity to alter the path, it becomes harder for it to say the harm merely happened.

## 3.4 The Gate Between Selection and Responsibility

Selection is not yet responsibility.

A fish selects. A dog selects. A market selects. A model selects. A bureaucracy selects. A frightened person selects. A traumatised person selects under pressure. A child selects before full understanding.

Mechanical Ethics must not collapse all of that into blame.

The gate to responsibility needs more structure:

```trace
responsibility_pressure :=
  visibility
  + decision_time_evidence
  + role_connection
  + meaningful_control
  + capacity_to_act
  + time_to_correct
```

Responsibility increases when an actor could see enough, had a relevant role, had enough control, had capacity to act, and had time to alter the path before hardening.

Responsibility weakens when those conditions are absent, contaminated, coerced, developmentally unavailable, or destroyed by circumstance.

This is why Mechanical Ethics will later say: visibility is not blame. Prediction is not destiny. Foresight is not omniscience.

But it will also say: delegation is not erasure. Automation is not absolution. Complexity is not a solvent for answerability.

The gate matters because without it, the book becomes either cruel or useless.

Too much responsibility, and every effect becomes guilt.

Too little responsibility, and every system becomes weather.

## 3.5 Local Reward Pull

Every acting system has some pull.

It may be hunger, fear, affection, profit, attention, status, votes, closure, relief, accuracy, speed, obedience, growth, or survival. The pull may be explicit or hidden. It may be designed or evolved. It may be conscious or mechanical.

```trace
local_reward_pull :=
  local_reward
  + selection_pull
  + repetition_tendency
```

Local reward is not the enemy.

A world without pull would not act. Organisms would not eat. People would not seek each other. Institutions would not maintain routines. Systems would not learn. A child would not play. A dog would not return to the person who loves it. A person in despair would not follow a tiny thread of meaning toward tomorrow.

The problem is misalignment between the local pull and the wider field of continuation.

A company rewarded for speed may stop seeing correction. A government rewarded for apparent control may hide burden. A platform rewarded for attention may make anger delicious. A model rewarded for prediction may learn correlations that narrow real lives. A person rewarded by immediate relief may damage the future they still need.

Mechanical Ethics does not say: remove reward.

It says: inspect what reward is training.

```trace
reward_question :=
  what_is_being_repeated
  + who_benefits
  + who_carries_cost
  + what_future_space_changes
  + can_correction_still_arrive
```

This becomes central later when the book turns to markets, institutions, platforms, artificial systems, and civilization.

## 3.5.1 Reward Is Not Structural Good

The book needs this distinction to remain load-bearing:

```trace
local_reward != structural_good
local_pain != structural_bad
```

Reward can point toward viable becoming. It can also pull away from it. Pain can signal damage. It can also accompany growth, warning, learning, grief, repair, or restraint. Mechanical Ethics cannot treat immediate pull as final value.

A healthy reward is one that remains aligned enough with continuation, orientation, learning, relation, repairability, and shared future-space.

```trace
healthy_reward :=
  local_reward
  aligned_enough_with
  continuation + learning + trust + repairability + shared_future_space
```

A false good is different. It appears locally attractive while corrupting the conditions of viable becoming.

```trace
false_good_patterns :=
  pleasure_that_addicts
  + efficiency_that_erases
  + safety_that_dominates
  + certainty_that_blinds
  + repair_that_only_changes_record
  + benefit_that_hides_burden
  + freedom_that_removes_correction
```

This is why the selective layer matters for good as well as harm. A system can learn to produce a local good and still damage the wider field. It can also learn a real good: feeding, teaching, warning, protecting, including, restoring, coordinating, or keeping repair reachable.

The question is not whether there is reward. The question is what form of becoming the reward is training.

## 3.6 When Systems Learn the Wrong Thing

A system can learn the wrong thing without being stupid.

It can become more efficient at producing an outcome that should never have been rewarded. It can become more stable by routing instability onto someone else. It can become more accurate against a proxy while becoming less truthful about the thing the proxy was meant to protect.

This is one of the deepest mechanical failures.

```trace
wrong_learning :=
  reward_signal
  improves_local_performance
  while
  narrowing_external_future_space
  or
  reducing_correction_capacity
```

The system does not need to hate anyone. It does not need to intend the residue. It does not need to know the person harmed. It may even describe itself as fair, efficient, neutral, or evidence-led.

A welfare system can learn suspicion. A prison system can learn containment. A school system can learn exclusion. A hospital can learn throughput. A platform can learn outrage. A legal process can learn delay. A model can learn the shadow of a bias and call it signal.

The question is not only what the system says it values.

The question is what it is being trained to preserve.

If it preserves its own throughput by making correction unreachable, it is learning the wrong thing. If it preserves its own authority by making contradiction expensive, it is learning the wrong thing. If it preserves its own confidence by demoting affected scopes, it is learning the wrong thing.

Part 3 ends here because the next problem is no longer action in general. It is action over time.

The book now has three layers:

```trace
layers_so_far :=
  causal_path
  + selective_pull
  + reflective_answerability
```

The next question is what these layers do to future-space when time starts closing doors.
