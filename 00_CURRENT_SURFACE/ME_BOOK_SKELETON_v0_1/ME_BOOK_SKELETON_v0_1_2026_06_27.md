# Mechanical Ethics Book Skeleton v0.1 — 2026-06-27

Status: first skeleton. Not finished prose. Not canon. Not validation.

Purpose: test the movement of the new Mechanical Ethics book from the simplest middle-out premise to the best current prescriptive answers before drafting full chapters.

## 0. Skeleton rule

```trace
skeleton_rule :=
  show_argument_motion
  + preserve_middle_out_order
  + mark_open_wounds
  + avoid_finished_style
```

This skeleton is not trying to sound good yet. It is trying to reveal whether the book has a real spine.

## 1. Whole-book compression

```trace
ME_book :=
  minimal_origin
  -> interaction
  -> layers
  -> future_space_and_time
  -> TRACE_ME_interface
  -> anti_laundering_gates
  -> protection_and_scope
  -> correction_and_residue
  -> dirty_conflict
  -> responsibility_after_visibility
  -> power_and_coercion
  -> civilization
  -> artificial_systems
  -> use_protocols
  -> limits
```

Plain English:

The book begins with a reader as an acting entity under uncertainty. It then shows that action changes a shared world where other entities also continue. Because time closes paths, correction can arrive too late, opportunity can expire, and residue can remain. From those pressures, the book earns its first constraints. It then builds duties, responsibility rules, dirty-conflict handling, legitimacy questions, and civilization-scale principles. It ends with limits rather than triumph.

## 2. The path the reader should experience

```trace
reader_path :=
  "I am here"
  -> "I can affect things"
  -> "I am not alone"
  -> "my action changes future-space"
  -> "uncertainty and time make this dangerous"
  -> "some things must not be hidden"
  -> "some protections and duties follow"
  -> "some conflicts remain dirty"
  -> "power must answer"
  -> "civilization is correctability under uncertainty"
```

If a reader cannot follow that path without already knowing TRACE, the book has failed its human-facing task.

## 3. Part 0 — How to Read This Book

Function: install safety rails before the argument begins.

The book tells the reader that Mechanical Ethics is not a moral oracle. It is a human-facing protocol for constrained action after honest seeing. It may recommend, constrain, halt, escalate, or record duties, but it does not discharge the actor's responsibility.

Chapters:

```text
0.1 This Is Not a Moral Oracle
0.2 TRACE and Mechanical Ethics
0.3 What This Book Can Do
0.4 What This Book Refuses to Do
0.5 How to Use the Numbered Protocols
```

Chapter functions:

- `0.1` blocks oracle-reading and automatic-moral-engine drift.
- `0.2` explains that TRACE describes and ME prescribes, while shared primitives remain TRACE-owned.
- `0.3` says ME can structure duties, constraints, repair, residue, escalation, and refusal.
- `0.4` says ME cannot remove hard choice, settle all value conflicts, validate TRACE, or become final moral law.
- `0.5` teaches the reader how to read numbered protocols as auditable findings, not permission slips.

The book should not open by declaring ethics. It should open by placing the reader inside action. The reader is not assumed to be good, rational, human, lawful, or deserving. The reader is something that can register, select, and change state under uncertainty.

First prose seed, not final:

> Something is here. It does not see everything. It can still change something. Time moves whether it chooses or refuses to choose. That is enough for the first problem.

## 6. Part 3 — Layers of Action

Function: distinguish causal, selective, and reflective layers.

The book must show that not every effect is the same kind of action. Rocks cause. Organisms select. Institutions, animals, humans, and artificial systems may preserve patterns, repeat, learn, model, or reflect. Responsibility does not attach merely because something caused an effect. It begins to press where selection, control, foresight, and correction become available.

Chapters:

```text
3.1 The Causal Layer
3.2 The Selective Layer
3.3 The Reflective Layer
3.4 The Gate Between Selection and Responsibility
3.5 Local Reward Pull
3.6 When Systems Learn the Wrong Thing
```

Chapter functions:

- `3.1` says causal effect is necessary but not enough for responsibility.
- `3.2` introduces selection, avoidance, pursuit, repetition, adaptation, and learning.
- `3.3` introduces self-models, other-models, reasons, time, and answerability.
- `3.4` marks the gate where responsibility pressure begins.
- `3.5` imports local reward pull without making appetite immoral.
- `3.6` shows why systems can become effective and harmful at the same time.


## 7. Part 4 — Harm, Time, and Future-Space

Function: give the reader the shared descriptive grammar without redefining TRACE.

Part 4 must quote TRACE-owned primitives. It may explain them in human language. It may not redefine them.

```trace
F_x(t) := (O_x(t), R_x(t), I_x(t))

O := option_topology
R := practical_reachability
I := usable_navigational_information
```

The book then explains: harm is not only pain. Harm can be narrowing of options, reachability, or usable information. Benefit can be expansion. Some changes are mixed, unknown, or incomparable. ME must not fake a scalar where only qualitative comparison exists.

Chapters:

```text
4.1 Future-Space
4.2 Options, Reachability, and Information
4.3 Harm as Narrowing
4.4 Benefit as Expansion
4.5 Loss Clocks
4.6 Opportunity Clocks
4.7 Correction That Arrives Too Late
4.8 Opportunity That Arrives Too Late
```

Chapter functions:

- `4.1` introduces future-space as the shared field of possible continuation.
- `4.2` separates options, reachability, and navigational information.
- `4.3` shows harm as contraction without reducing harm to pain.
- `4.4` shows expansion without treating every expansion as justified.
- `4.5` introduces loss clocks.
- `4.6` introduces opportunity clocks.
- `4.7` converts late correction into ethical pressure.
- `4.8` converts expired opportunity into ethical pressure.
