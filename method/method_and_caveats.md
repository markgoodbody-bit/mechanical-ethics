# Method and Caveats

## Method

The Correction Window Test studies transitions, not labels.

Instead of asking only whether a system has an appeal, audit, oversight route, or human review, it asks whether that route can still affect the outcome before the consequence becomes difficult or impossible to reverse.

The basic method is:

1. identify the selection point;
2. identify the affected person or group;
3. identify the hardening point;
4. identify the correction carrier;
5. compare correction time against hardening time;
6. state what remains uncertain.

## Why timing matters

A safeguard can exist and still fail.

Examples:

- an appeal exists, but the person loses housing before it concludes;
- a human reviewer exists, but cannot stop the action;
- an audit exists, but only after deployment;
- a record exists, but does not show what the affected person needs to challenge;
- a model warning exists, but reaches no brake;
- a complaint route exists, but the relevant opportunity has already closed.

The test does not say timing is the whole of ethics. It says timing is a necessary condition for live correction.

## Terms

### Hardening

A consequence hardens when ordinary correction no longer restores the original path.

Hardening can be legal, practical, financial, social, technical, clinical, informational, or institutional.

### Carrier

A carrier is the mechanism that makes a safeguard real: authority, resources, deadline, evidence access, pause power, reversal power, compensation, protection, or enforcement.

A policy statement without a carrier is weak.

### Live protection

A safeguard is live protection when it can still alter the affected person's path in time.

### After-the-fact review

After-the-fact review may still matter. It can expose failure, correct records, compensate, sanction, or prevent recurrence.

But it should not be confused with protection that could have prevented or reversed the original live consequence.

## Caveats

### 1. Faster is not always better

Fast systems can be dangerous if they remove evidence, standing, appeal, or public notice.

Good speed removes dead time.

Bad speed removes protection.

### 2. Not every harm is timing-sensitive

Some harms are mainly about distribution, legitimacy, discrimination, coercion, rights, dignity, or material deprivation. Timing may still matter, but it is not always the central issue.

### 3. Unknown is not reassurance

If the public record does not show whether a safeguard can act in time, the result should be Unknown, not Safe.

### 4. Public records are partial

Public records may show intake, completion, backlog, or deadlines without showing lived burden, case quality, closure type, or whether affected people were restored.

### 5. Segment clocks are not end-to-end repair clocks

A complaint-stage series is not the whole correction route.

For example, a regulator's complaint-handling time does not include the original request, internal review, compliance after decision, tribunal appeal, or the affected person's practical deadline unless those are separately measured.

### 6. No basket without additivity proof

Do not add categories into a total repair budget unless the source classification supports it.

A source can be useful without being additive.

### 7. No clock without a series

One number may provide context. It does not establish direction.

Claims about pressure, decline, recovery, or outbidding require a series that others can inspect and extend.

## Claim ceiling

Allowed claims:

- This test helps inspect whether a safeguard can act in time.
- Some systems have formal safeguards that may not be live protection.
- Public-record series can help test whether correction channels are under pressure.
- Unknown results are useful when they prevent false reassurance.

Disallowed claims:

- Mechanical Ethics is validated.
- This test proves a system is ethical.
- AI alignment is solved.
- Timing explains all institutional failure.
- A local public-record surface proves a general theory.

## Preferred public wording

Use:

> This is a practical diagnostic for checking whether correction can still matter before harm hardens.

Avoid:

> This is a new complete ethics framework.

Avoid:

> This proves current safeguards are fake.

Avoid:

> This solves AI governance.

## Publication discipline

Every public surface should include:

- data or examples;
- source notes;
- caveats;
- falsifiers;
- claim limits;
- update history.

The goal is not persuasion by compression. The goal is checkable work.
