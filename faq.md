# FAQ

## Is this a complete ethics framework?

No.

This repository currently presents one public diagnostic: the Correction Window Test.

It asks whether correction can reach an affected person before a consequence becomes hard to reverse.

## Does this prove Mechanical Ethics is valid?

No.

The test is a practical tool. It may be useful, weak, incomplete, or already covered better by existing methods. That is why the repository includes falsifiers.

## Is this an AI alignment solution?

No.

It may be relevant to AI alignment because AI systems increasingly make or shape selections that enter the world. But this test does not solve alignment.

A safer claim is:

> Correction capacity should not fall behind system capability.

## Why focus on timing?

Because many safeguards fail not because they are absent, but because they arrive too late.

An appeal, audit, complaint route, human review, or override may still matter after harm. But it is not the same as protection that can change the affected person's path before the consequence hardens.

## Does the test say faster systems are always better?

No.

Fast systems can be dangerous if they remove notice, evidence, appeal, standing, or public accountability.

Good speed removes dead time.

Bad speed removes protection.

## What is a hardening point?

A hardening point is when ordinary correction no longer restores the original path.

Examples include a job being filled, a payment stopping, rent being missed, a medical window closing, a label being copied downstream, or evidence being lost.

## What is a correction carrier?

A correction carrier is the mechanism that gives a safeguard real force.

Examples include pause power, reversal power, evidence access, affected-person standing, a deadline for the corrector, technical rollback, independent review, or compensation authority.

## Why not just say "appeal" or "human review"?

Because those words often hide the decisive question.

Can the appeal or human reviewer still change the outcome in time?

A human who cannot see, stop, reverse, or escalate the decision before hardening is not live protection.

## Can the test return Unknown?

Yes.

Unknown is often the most honest result.

If public records do not show timing, evidence access, standing, authority, or actual correction outcomes, the test should not infer safety or failure.

## Is this anti-AI?

No.

The test can support responsible AI use by showing what correction machinery needs to exist before a system is deployed into consequential settings.

The point is not "do not use AI." The point is "do not scale action faster than correction can operate."

## Is this only about public services?

No.

The test can be applied to public services, companies, AI agents, medical systems, hiring tools, credit systems, content moderation, education, housing, software deployment, and other decision routes.

## Can this be used as certification?

No.

A positive result means only that a safeguard appears to have a live correction route on the available evidence.

It does not mean the system is fair, lawful, safe, legitimate, unbiased, or aligned.

## What would make the test fail as a public tool?

The test should be weakened or revised if:

- strangers cannot apply it;
- it adds nothing beyond existing checklists;
- it treats every issue as timing;
- it overreads missing evidence;
- it cannot distinguish live protection from after-the-fact review;
- it becomes a validation claim for a larger theory.
